# Weight Loss Medications: FDA-Approved Options, How They Work, and What to Expect

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 24, 2026*

Weight loss medications have changed dramatically over the past several years. The arrival of GLP-1 receptor agonists — drugs originally developed for diabetes that proved to be powerful appetite suppressants — reshaped the conversation around pharmacological weight management. These drugs produce results that were previously only achievable with surgery, and they've moved from niche prescriptions to one of the most discussed topics in medicine.

This guide covers what's currently FDA-approved for weight loss, how each class of medication works, typical outcomes, who qualifies, and what the limitations and risks look like.

> **Medical disclaimer:** This article is for informational purposes only. Weight loss medications require a prescription and medical supervision. Talk to your doctor to determine whether medication is appropriate for your situation.

## Why Medications for Weight Loss?

Obesity is classified as a chronic disease by the American Medical Association and most major medical societies. For many people, lifestyle interventions alone — diet and exercise — produce modest results that are difficult to sustain long-term. Biological factors including genetics, hormonal regulation of appetite, and metabolic adaptation mean that willpower is often fighting against significant physiological resistance.

Weight loss medications work by modifying some of these underlying mechanisms. They are not shortcuts — they work best in combination with lifestyle changes — but for qualifying patients, they can produce meaningful, sustained weight loss that improves health outcomes including blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol, and cardiovascular risk.

## FDA-Approved Weight Loss Medications

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (The New Standard)

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) receptor agonists are the most effective weight loss medications currently available. They mimic a naturally occurring gut hormone that regulates appetite, slows gastric emptying, and reduces food intake.

**Semaglutide (Wegovy)**

Wegovy is the highest-dose semaglutide formulation approved specifically for chronic weight management. It is the same molecule as Ozempic (used for Type 2 diabetes), but at higher doses.

- **How it's taken:** Weekly subcutaneous injection
- **Typical weight loss:** Clinical trials showed an average weight loss of **14.9% of body weight** over 68 weeks (STEP 1 trial). In practice, results range from 8% to 20%+ depending on adherence and individual response
- **How it works:** Slows gastric emptying (food stays in the stomach longer, reducing hunger), acts on brain appetite centers to reduce food cravings and reward-driven eating
- **Who qualifies:** BMI ≥ 30, or BMI ≥ 27 with at least one weight-related condition (high blood pressure, Type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, etc.)
- **Common side effects:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation — most common during dose escalation, typically improve with time
- **Serious but rare risks:** Pancreatitis, gallbladder disease; contraindicated in patients with personal or family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN2 syndrome

**Tirzepatide (Zepbound)**

Zepbound is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist — it activates two pathways instead of one — which makes it the most effective weight loss medication available as of 2026.

- **How it's taken:** Weekly subcutaneous injection
- **Typical weight loss:** Clinical trials showed an average weight loss of **20.9% of body weight** at the highest dose (15 mg) over 72 weeks (SURMOUNT-1 trial). This approaches outcomes seen with bariatric surgery in some patients
- **How it works:** Dual-action on both GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide) and GLP-1 receptors provides stronger appetite suppression and metabolic effects than GLP-1 alone
- **Who qualifies:** Same criteria as Wegovy (BMI ≥ 30, or BMI ≥ 27 with comorbidity)
- **Common side effects:** Similar to semaglutide — nausea, GI symptoms during escalation
- **Note:** Tirzepatide is also approved as Mounjaro for Type 2 diabetes. Zepbound is the weight management indication

For a detailed comparison of these two drugs, see our [Ozempic vs Wegovy breakdown](/compare/ozempic-vs-wegovy) and [Ozempic vs Mounjaro comparison](/compare/ozempic-vs-mounjaro).

**Liraglutide (Saxenda)**

An older GLP-1 agonist, now largely superseded by semaglutide in clinical practice but still available.

- **How it's taken:** Daily injection (vs. weekly for Wegovy/Zepbound)
- **Typical weight loss:** About **8% of body weight** on average over 56 weeks
- **Why it's used less now:** Lower efficacy than semaglutide, and daily injections are less convenient than weekly; also more expensive for the level of effect

### Orlistat (Alli, Xenical)

Orlistat is the only FDA-approved weight loss medication that works in the gut rather than the brain. It blocks the enzyme lipase, preventing the absorption of about 30% of dietary fat.

- **How it's taken:** Oral capsule, three times daily with meals
- **Typical weight loss:** About **5–7% of body weight** over one year — modest compared to GLP-1 agents
- **Available forms:** Xenical (prescription, 120 mg) and Alli (OTC, 60 mg)
- **Key requirement:** You must follow a low-fat diet — typically less than 30% of calories from fat. Eating high-fat meals with orlistat causes oily, loose stools, oily spotting, and urgency that most people find unacceptable
- **Side effects:** The GI effects are the main issue. Many patients stop due to embarrassing accidents
- **Who it works for:** Patients willing to commit to a consistently low-fat diet who want an oral option without GLP-1 side effects

### Phentermine-Topiramate ER (Qsymia)

A combination pill approved for long-term weight management.

- **How it's taken:** Daily oral capsule
- **Typical weight loss:** About **9–10% of body weight** at the highest dose
- **How it works:** Phentermine is a stimulant (amphetamine-related) that suppresses appetite; topiramate is an anticonvulsant that also reduces appetite through a different mechanism
- **Contraindications:** Cannot be used during pregnancy (topiramate causes birth defects), in patients with heart disease, hyperthyroidism, or recent MAOI use
- **Side effects:** Dry mouth, constipation, tingling sensations, insomnia, cognitive effects ("brain fog")
- **Schedule:** Phentermine is a Schedule IV controlled substance, so prescribing has some restrictions

### Naltrexone-Bupropion ER (Contrave)

Another combination pill that acts on reward pathways in the brain.

- **How it's taken:** Oral tablet, twice daily
- **Typical weight loss:** About **8–9% of body weight** over one year
- **How it works:** Naltrexone blocks opioid receptors (dampening food reward signals); bupropion is an antidepressant/smoking cessation drug that also affects appetite
- **Contraindications:** Cannot be used with other opioids (blocks their effect), in patients with seizure disorders, eating disorders, or recent MAOI use
- **Side effects:** Nausea, constipation, headache, elevated blood pressure (requires monitoring)
- **Black box warning:** Like other bupropion-containing products, carries a warning about suicidal thoughts in young patients

### Phentermine (Alone, Short-Term)

Phentermine is the oldest and most widely prescribed weight loss medication in the US. However, it is FDA-approved only for **short-term use** (typically up to 12 weeks).

- **How it's taken:** Daily oral tablet or capsule
- **Typical weight loss:** Modest and short-term — most benefit occurs in the first few weeks
- **Mechanism:** Stimulant that suppresses appetite by releasing norepinephrine
- **Limitations:** Tolerance develops, effectiveness wanes, and the weight typically returns after stopping. Not a long-term solution

## How to Qualify for Weight Loss Medication

Most FDA-approved weight loss medications have similar eligibility criteria:

- **BMI ≥ 30** (obesity), regardless of other conditions
- **BMI ≥ 27** with at least one weight-related condition (Type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, sleep apnea, dyslipidemia, cardiovascular disease, or others)

Your doctor will also evaluate:
- Current medications (many interactions apply)
- Pregnancy status or plans
- Mental health history (relevant for Contrave)
- Personal and family medical history
- Cardiac and renal function

## Medication vs. Surgery: How They Compare

| | GLP-1 Medications | Bariatric Surgery |
|---|---|---|
| Average weight loss | 15–21% | 25–35% |
| Durability | Requires ongoing medication | Generally durable long-term |
| Reversibility | Stop medication = weight typically returns | Largely permanent |
| Risk | Low (mainly GI side effects) | Surgical risk, nutritional deficiencies |
| Cost | $900–$1,500/month (before insurance) | $15,000–$25,000 one-time |
| Access | Prescription required | Specialized centers, surgery |

The gap between medication efficacy and surgical outcomes is narrowing, particularly with tirzepatide. Some clinical researchers now estimate that tirzepatide approaches the efficacy of older bariatric procedures like laparoscopic banding.

## What Results to Realistically Expect

Several important caveats about weight loss medication outcomes:

**The "average" obscures wide variation.** Clinical trial averages include both strong responders and non-responders. Some people lose 25%+ of body weight on semaglutide; others lose less than 5%. Your result is difficult to predict in advance.

**Weight loss slows over time.** The first 16–24 weeks typically show the fastest loss. After that, the rate slows as the body adapts. Most trials plateau around week 60–72.

**Weight returns when you stop.** This is a critical point: obesity is a chronic condition, and GLP-1 medications treat it as such. Stopping the medication — without sustained lifestyle changes — results in regaining a significant portion of the lost weight within 12 months. The STEP 4 trial showed that patients who stopped semaglutide regained about two-thirds of their lost weight within a year.

**Side effects are real.** Nausea affects 40–50% of GLP-1 patients, particularly during dose escalation. Most patients adapt, but some cannot tolerate the medication.

## Insurance Coverage and Cost

Weight loss medications are expensive out-of-pocket:
- **Wegovy**: ~$1,350/month list price
- **Zepbound**: ~$1,060/month list price
- **Qsymia/Contrave**: ~$200–$400/month
- **Orlistat (Alli OTC)**: ~$50–$80/month

**Insurance coverage** is inconsistent. Medicare did not cover obesity medications for most of 2024–2025, though this has been changing. Many commercial plans have added GLP-1 coverage with prior authorization requirements. Manufacturer savings cards can significantly reduce out-of-pocket costs for commercially insured patients.

The cost-benefit equation is compelling from a healthcare perspective — obesity-related conditions cost far more to treat than the medications — but insurance coverage gaps remain a significant access barrier.

## The Bottom Line

Weight loss medications have become significantly more effective in recent years, with GLP-1 and dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonists achieving weight loss that was previously only possible with surgery. Tirzepatide (Zepbound) leads the current field in efficacy, followed closely by semaglutide (Wegovy).

These medications are not for everyone. They require a prescription, ongoing monitoring, meaningful lifestyle changes to maximize and sustain results, and — critically — long-term commitment to the medication to avoid weight regain. For patients who qualify and can access them, they represent a genuine step forward in treating a chronic disease that carries serious health consequences.

If you're considering a GLP-1 medication, the starting point is a conversation with your primary care physician or an obesity medicine specialist to determine whether you're a candidate and which option fits your health history and goals.
