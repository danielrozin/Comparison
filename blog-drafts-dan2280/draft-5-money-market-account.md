# Money Market Account: How It Works, Current Rates, and Whether It's Right for You

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 25, 2026*

A money market account (MMA) is a type of deposit account that typically pays more interest than a standard checking or savings account while offering some features — like check-writing or a debit card — that standard savings accounts don't. It occupies a middle ground between a savings account (maximize interest, limited access) and a checking account (maximum access, minimal interest).

Understanding what a money market account actually is, what separates a good one from a mediocre one, and how it compares to alternatives like high yield savings accounts and CDs can help you put your cash to work more effectively.

## What Is a Money Market Account?

A money market account is a federally insured deposit account that typically offers:
- **Higher interest rates** than standard savings accounts (though comparable to the best high yield savings accounts)
- **Check-writing privileges** — you can write a limited number of checks per month
- **Debit card access** — some MMAs include a debit card for ATM withdrawals or point-of-sale purchases
- **FDIC or NCUA insurance** up to $250,000 per depositor per institution

The "money market" name originally referred to the fact that some banks invest MMA deposits in short-term, low-risk instruments — Treasury bills, commercial paper, certificates of deposit — to generate the interest they pass on to depositors. Consumers don't need to understand the underlying mechanics; what matters is the rate and features.

**Important distinction:** A money market *account* (MMA) is a bank deposit account. A money market *fund* (MMF) is a mutual fund product sold through brokerages. The two are often confused but function differently — money market funds are not FDIC-insured.

## How Money Market Accounts Work

### Interest and APY

Like savings accounts, MMAs earn interest calculated as a percentage of your balance, expressed as APY (Annual Percentage Yield). The rate is almost always variable — it can change as the Federal Reserve moves the federal funds rate.

Interest typically compounds daily and credits monthly, though some institutions compound monthly. The APY calculation includes compounding effects, making it the most useful number for comparison.

### Check-Writing and Debit Access

Not all MMAs offer check-writing, but many do. Federal Regulation D historically limited savings accounts (including MMAs) to six withdrawals per month — a rule the Fed suspended in 2020 but many banks still apply. Some institutions allow unlimited withdrawals; others still enforce the six-transaction limit with fees for exceeding it.

If you need frequent access to your funds, confirm the withdrawal policy before opening an account.

### Minimum Balance Requirements

Money market accounts typically carry higher minimum balance requirements than standard savings accounts:
- **To open:** Often $500–$2,500; some require $10,000 or more
- **To earn the advertised APY:** Some accounts pay the top rate only above a minimum balance threshold and a lower rate below it
- **To avoid fees:** Many MMAs charge a monthly fee if your balance falls below a minimum — commonly $5–$25/month if the balance drops below $2,500–$25,000

Always read the fee structure carefully. An MMA with a $1,000/month fee on balances below $10,000 is only attractive if you can reliably maintain that balance.

## Current Money Market Account Rates

As of mid-2026, the best money market accounts from online banks and credit unions offer rates competitive with the best high yield savings accounts — in the **4.50%–5.25% APY range**. Traditional brick-and-mortar banks continue to offer much lower rates, typically 0.01%–0.50% APY.

**Where the best rates come from:**
- **Online banks:** Ally Bank, Marcus by Goldman Sachs, Discover, CIT Bank, Synchrony, LendingClub
- **Credit unions:** Some offer strong rates, especially for members
- **Fintechs:** Various platforms offer competitive MMAs, sometimes with FDIC passthrough insurance via bank partners

Rate comparison sites like Bankrate and NerdWallet track current rates daily and let you filter by minimum deposit and features.

## Money Market Account vs. High Yield Savings Account

This is the most common comparison. Here's how they stack up:

| | Money Market Account | High Yield Savings Account |
|---|---|---|
| **Typical APY** | 4.50%–5.25% | 4.50%–5.25% |
| **FDIC/NCUA insured** | Yes | Yes |
| **Check writing** | Often yes | Almost never |
| **Debit card** | Sometimes | Rarely |
| **Minimum balance** | Often $1,000–$25,000 | Often $0–$1,000 |
| **Withdrawal limits** | Bank-specific | Bank-specific |
| **Mobile app/online** | Depends on bank | Usually yes |

**The verdict:** For most people with less than $10,000 to deposit, a high yield savings account is simpler — lower minimums, no fee risk if the balance dips, and equivalent rates. For people with larger cash balances who want occasional check-writing convenience, an MMA may be the better fit.

## Money Market Account vs. Checking Account

| | Money Market Account | Checking Account |
|---|---|---|
| **Interest earned** | Yes (meaningful) | Rarely (minimal if any) |
| **Debit card** | Sometimes | Always |
| **Check writing** | Limited | Unlimited |
| **ATM access** | Sometimes | Almost always |
| **Minimum balance** | Often higher | Often lower or zero |
| **Monthly fees** | Yes, if balance drops | Varies |

For the [full Checking vs Savings Account comparison](/compare/checking-account-vs-savings-account), including the tradeoffs in access, interest, and features, see our detailed breakdown.

An MMA is not a replacement for a checking account. It doesn't have the unlimited transaction access and bill pay features you need for daily spending. The typical pattern: keep 1–2 months of expenses in checking for daily use, and put the rest in an MMA or HYSA.

## Money Market Account vs. CD

| | Money Market Account | CD |
|---|---|---|
| **Rate** | Variable | Fixed for term |
| **Liquidity** | Full access (subject to limits) | Locked until maturity |
| **Early withdrawal penalty** | None | Yes (typically 60–180 days interest) |
| **Rate risk** | Falls if Fed cuts rates | Locked in at opening rate |
| **Best when** | You need flexibility | You don't need the money + want rate certainty |

The decision between an MMA and a CD often comes down to your rate outlook and how soon you'll need the money. If you believe rates will fall, locking in a CD rate now may be advantageous. If you need the money within 12–18 months or want to keep your options open, an MMA wins on flexibility.

## Who Should Use a Money Market Account?

**Good fit:**
- Someone with a larger cash reserve ($10,000–$100,000+) who wants to earn meaningful interest while retaining the ability to write occasional checks
- A small business owner who wants to earn interest on operating cash between expenses
- A retiree who needs periodic checks written from savings for healthcare, HOA fees, or estate-related expenses
- Anyone who has already maxed out high yield savings options at one institution and wants to diversify across banks

**Not the best fit:**
- Someone with less than $5,000 to deposit who can't maintain the minimum balance (fees will eat the interest advantage)
- Someone who needs daily access to their funds (a checking account serves this better)
- Someone looking to beat inflation over the long term (a MMA is not a substitute for investing)

## How to Choose a Money Market Account

**Priority checklist:**

1. **APY** — Confirm the current rate and whether it applies to your expected balance tier. Some MMAs pay tiered rates (higher on balances above $10,000 or $25,000).

2. **Minimum balance requirements** — Know both the opening minimum and the ongoing minimum to earn the top APY and avoid fees. Be honest with yourself about whether you'll maintain it.

3. **FDIC/NCUA insurance** — Non-negotiable. Verify the account is federally insured.

4. **Monthly fees** — Look for no monthly fee, or know exactly what balance keeps the fee waived.

5. **Check writing** — If you want it, confirm the bank includes it and understand how many checks are allowed per month.

6. **Debit card/ATM access** — If you want it, confirm it's included. Note that frequent ATM access from a savings-type account can burn through your withdrawal limit quickly.

7. **Transfer speed** — For emergency funds, confirm how long it takes to move money to your checking account. Standard ACH takes 1–3 business days.

8. **Institution reputation** — Online reviews, CFPB complaint database, and J.D. Power ratings give you a sense of customer service quality, which matters when something goes wrong.

## Opening a Money Market Account

The process at most online banks takes 10–20 minutes:

1. **Compare current rates** across multiple institutions using a rate comparison site
2. **Choose an institution** that meets your balance, rate, and feature requirements
3. **Gather your information:** SSN, government ID, current address, existing bank account for the initial transfer
4. **Complete the online application** — basic identity verification, sometimes a soft credit pull (doesn't affect credit score)
5. **Link your checking account** for transfers
6. **Fund the account** — the opening deposit typically settles within 1–3 business days
7. **Set up automatic savings transfers** if building toward a goal

## The Bottom Line

A money market account is a solid place to keep cash that needs to stay liquid but doesn't need to be available the same day. The interest rates at the best online banks rival those of high yield savings accounts, and the added convenience of occasional check-writing makes MMAs genuinely useful for some account holders.

The key trade-off is minimum balance — MMAs reward larger deposits and penalize accounts that fall below the threshold. If you can comfortably maintain the minimum and the check-writing feature matters to you, an MMA from an online bank is a competitive choice. If you're depositing less than $5,000 or want the simplicity of no minimums, a high yield savings account will likely serve you just as well.

The practical step: if you're holding cash in a traditional savings account right now, either an MMA or a HYSA from an online bank will earn you significantly more interest with the same federal insurance protection. The difference between them is mostly about features and minimums, not risk.
