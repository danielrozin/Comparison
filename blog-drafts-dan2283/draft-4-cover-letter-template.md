# Cover Letter Template: The Structure That Works, What to Write in Each Section, and Mistakes to Avoid

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 24, 2026*

A cover letter is a one-page document that accompanies your resume when applying for a job. Its purpose is not to repeat your resume — it's to explain *why* you want this specific role at this specific company and to demonstrate that you can communicate in writing. Done well, a cover letter gives a hiring manager a reason to move your application to the interview pile. Done poorly, it raises doubts that your resume alone wouldn't.

This guide provides a ready-to-use cover letter template, explains what to write in each section, shows a filled-in example, and walks through the mistakes that cause otherwise strong candidates to get overlooked.

---

## The Cover Letter Template

Use this as your starting framework. Customize the bracketed fields before sending.

---

**[Your Full Name]**  
[City, State] · [Phone Number] · [Email Address] · [LinkedIn URL, optional]

[Date]

[Hiring Manager's Name] *(or "Hiring Team" if unknown)*  
[Job Title They Hold, e.g., Recruiting Manager]  
[Company Name]  
[Company Address, optional]

---

**Re: [Job Title] — [Job Requisition Number, if provided]**

Dear [Hiring Manager's Name] *(or "Dear Hiring Team"):*

**Opening paragraph (3–4 sentences):**  
State the role you're applying for and where you found it. Lead with one compelling sentence about why you're applying — not "I am excited to apply," but something specific: a product you use, a mission you believe in, or a recent milestone by the company that caught your attention. Close the paragraph with a one-line statement of your most relevant qualification.

**Body paragraph 1 (4–5 sentences):**  
Describe one or two specific accomplishments from your background that are directly relevant to this role. Use numbers where possible — revenue generated, percentage improvements, team size led, cost saved. Show that you understand what the role requires and connect your past work to that requirement.

**Body paragraph 2 (3–4 sentences, optional):**  
Address any gap, pivot, or context that isn't obvious from your resume but needs to be acknowledged. Alternatively, use this paragraph to show domain knowledge about the company's challenges or the industry — something that demonstrates you've done research and understand their world.

**Closing paragraph (2–3 sentences):**  
Express genuine interest in discussing the role further. Thank the reader for their time. Include a clear call to action — something like "I'd welcome the opportunity to speak with you about how my background can contribute to [specific team/goal]."

Sincerely,  
[Your Name]

## What to Write in Each Paragraph

### Opening Paragraph: Lead With a Hook, Not a Formula

The single most common mistake in cover letters is the weak opener:

> "I am writing to express my interest in the [Role] position at [Company], which I found posted on [Job Board]."

This sentence tells the reader nothing they don't already know. The job, the company, the job board — they're self-evident. It burns your first impression on noise.

**What to do instead:** Start with something specific. A product observation, a business problem you recognize, a piece of company news. Show that you're writing to this company — not copying a template you sent to 200 others.

**Examples of strong openers:**
- "After two years building fraud detection systems at Meridian Bank, reading about ClearPath's real-time risk scoring expansion felt like reading a job description written for my background specifically."
- "I've used Asana every day for three years. When I saw you're hiring a senior product manager to lead mobile workflows, I knew I had to apply — this is the product area I know most deeply."
- "The reason I want to join [Company] has less to do with the job description and more to do with the problem you're solving: [one sentence on the problem]."

### Body Paragraph 1: Numbers First, Story Second

Accomplishments without numbers are hard to evaluate. Numbers without context are hard to trust. The best body paragraphs combine both:

**Structure:** What you did → the specific number result → why it mattered or what it enabled.

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| "I led a team that improved customer retention." | "I led a 6-person team that reduced 30-day churn from 14% to 8%, adding an estimated $2.1M in annual recurring revenue." |
| "I have experience managing budgets." | "I managed a $4.5M annual marketing budget and reallocated 20% from underperforming paid channels to SEO, which drove a 65% increase in organic traffic over 12 months." |
| "I'm skilled at stakeholder management." | "I coordinated a cross-functional product launch with legal, finance, engineering, and marketing — moving from concept to live product in 9 weeks with zero scope compromises." |

### Body Paragraph 2: Research or Address the Elephant

Use the second body paragraph for one of two things:

**Option A — Show domain knowledge.** Name a challenge or trend in their industry that's relevant to the role. Reference something specific about their product or market position. This proves you did research and helps you stand out from candidates who sent form letters.

**Option B — Address a potential concern.** If you're changing industries, explain your relevant transferable skills. If you have a gap in employment, address it briefly and factually. If you're applying for a senior role from a more junior background, acknowledge it and make the case. Don't ignore the elephant — hiring managers will wonder, and a brief honest explanation is almost always better than silence.

### Closing Paragraph: Make It Easy to Say Yes

Don't end with "I hope to hear from you." That puts the friction on the reader. Instead, restate your enthusiasm briefly, thank them, and state your availability:

> "I'd welcome the opportunity to discuss how my background in [specific area] could contribute to [specific team or goal]. I'm available for a conversation at your convenience — please feel free to reach me at [phone] or [email]."

## Common Cover Letter Mistakes

**1. Talking about what the job would do for you, not what you'd do for them**  
"This role would give me the opportunity to grow my skills in X" focuses on your benefit. Flip it: what do you bring to their problem?

**2. Repeating the resume bullet by bullet**  
The cover letter should add context that the resume can't — motivation, personality, relevant backstory. If someone reads both documents and learns nothing new from the letter, it wasn't worth writing.

**3. Writing more than one page**  
One page. If your cover letter is longer, it doesn't signal thoroughness — it signals you can't prioritize.

**4. Generic openings**  
"I am excited to apply..." is a signal that the letter is templated. Hiring managers read thousands.

**5. Addressing it wrong**  
"To Whom It May Concern" is outdated. If you can't find the hiring manager's name after a LinkedIn search, "Dear Hiring Team" is better. If you find their name — use it.

**6. Focusing on passion, not performance**  
"I'm passionate about marketing" tells a hiring manager nothing. "I grew organic traffic 65% in 12 months by restructuring our content calendar" tells them a great deal. Lead with performance.

**7. Not tailoring it**  
The job description is a roadmap. It tells you exactly what the hiring manager wants. Mirror their language, address their stated needs, and show that you read it carefully.

## Cover Letter Format Checklist

Before sending, verify:

- [ ] Header includes your name, contact info, and the date
- [ ] Addressed to a named person (or "Hiring Team" — not "To Whom It May Concern")
- [ ] Opening sentence is specific and not a formula
- [ ] At least one accomplishment with a number
- [ ] One sentence showing research about the company
- [ ] Closing includes a call to action and your availability
- [ ] Under one page (ideally 3–4 paragraphs)
- [ ] Same font and formatting style as your resume
- [ ] No spelling or grammar errors (run spell check, then read it aloud)
- [ ] Saved as a PDF unless the job posting specifies otherwise

## Key Takeaways

- A cover letter's job is to explain **why you want this specific role at this specific company** — not to repeat your resume
- **Lead with a specific hook**, not a formula opener — show you're writing to this employer, not mass-applying
- **Use numbers** to describe accomplishments: not "I improved customer retention" but "I reduced 30-day churn from 14% to 8%"
- **Keep it to one page** — 3–4 paragraphs is the standard; anything longer signals poor editing
- **Tailor it to each job** — use their language from the job description; address the stated needs directly
- **Address concerns proactively** — a career pivot, employment gap, or junior-for-senior application is better explained briefly than left for the reader to wonder about
- End with a **clear call to action** — state your availability and make it easy for them to reply
