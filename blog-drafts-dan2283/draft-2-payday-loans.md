# Payday Loans: How They Work, What They Really Cost, and What to Do Instead

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 22, 2026*

A payday loan is a short-term loan — typically $100 to $1,000 — that you agree to repay in full when your next paycheck arrives, usually in two weeks. They're marketed as a quick fix for emergency expenses and are available from storefronts and online lenders with almost no credit requirements. The catch is the cost: the fees charged on payday loans translate to annual percentage rates (APRs) that frequently exceed **300%–400%**, and sometimes top 600%.

This guide explains exactly how payday loans work, what they actually cost in plain numbers, why they create debt cycles for many borrowers, and what alternatives exist if you need cash quickly.

---

## How a Payday Loan Works

The mechanics are straightforward:

1. **You apply** — in a storefront or online. Approval is almost instant. The lender typically requires proof of income, a bank account, and a government-issued ID. Credit checks are minimal or nonexistent.
2. **You receive the funds** — cash from a storefront, or an ACH deposit to your bank account (often same-day or next business day).
3. **You give a postdated check or ACH authorization** — the lender holds a check dated to your next payday for the full loan amount plus fees, or you authorize an automatic bank withdrawal.
4. **On your payday, the lender collects** — the check is cashed or the ACH withdrawal processes. If you can't cover it, the lender may offer a "rollover" — extending the loan for another fee.

### Typical Payday Loan Fee Structure

Fees are usually stated as a flat dollar amount per $100 borrowed. Common fee ranges:

| State/Context | Fee per $100 Borrowed | 2-Week APR |
|---|---|---|
| Average (where legal) | $15–$20 | 391%–521% |
| High-fee states | $25–$30 | 651%–782% |
| Online lenders | $15–$30 | 391%–782% |

**Example:** You borrow $300. The lender charges $15 per $100, so your fee is $45. In two weeks, you owe $345. If you can't pay and roll it over for another two weeks, you pay another $45 fee — now you've paid $90 in fees to borrow $300 for a month. The annualized cost of that $90 fee on a $300 loan for 30 days: **roughly 720% APR**.

## The Debt Trap: Why Rollovers Are So Costly

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) found that more than **80% of payday loans are rolled over or renewed within 14 days**, and that a majority of payday loan revenue comes from borrowers who take out 10 or more loans per year.

The math explains why: if you borrow $400 to cover rent and can't repay it all in two weeks, you pay a $60 fee to roll it over. Next payday, same problem — another $60 fee. After 8 rollovers (about 4 months), you've paid $480 in fees and still owe the original $400. The longer the loan extends, the more you pay in fees relative to what you borrowed.

### Why Payday Loans Affect Future Paychecks

Every time you take a payday loan, the repayment comes out of the next paycheck first — before rent, groceries, utilities, or other necessities. For borrowers living paycheck to paycheck, paying back the full loan plus fees often means the same shortfall appears the following month, driving another loan.

This is the structural debt trap: not predatory intent by the borrower, but a math problem. Borrowing against future income means less money in the next period, which creates the same shortage that triggered borrowing in the first place.

## Are Payday Loans Legal?

Payday loan regulation varies dramatically by state:

**States where payday lending is heavily restricted or banned (effective rate caps of 36% or lower):**
Arkansas, Arizona, Connecticut, Georgia, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, Vermont, West Virginia (and Washington D.C.)

**States with active payday lending (looser regulation):**
Alabama, Alaska, California, Colorado, Delaware, Florida, Hawaii, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Michigan, Minnesota, Mississippi, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Hampshire, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin, Wyoming

Some states that previously allowed high-rate payday lending have reformed — Colorado and Virginia, for example, passed rate cap laws that meaningfully reduced payday loan costs.

**Online payday lenders** sometimes operate across state lines using tribal affiliations or out-of-state charters to avoid state rate caps. Always check whether a lender is licensed in your state.

## 6 Alternatives to Payday Loans

If you're considering a payday loan because of a cash shortfall, these options are almost always less expensive:

### 1. Credit Union Payday Alternative Loan (PAL)

Federal credit unions offer **Payday Alternative Loans (PALs)** — regulated by the National Credit Union Administration. PAL I: $200–$1,000, max 28% APR, 1–6 month term. PAL II: up to $2,000, max 28% APR, up to 12 months. These carry a small application fee (up to $20) but are dramatically cheaper than payday loans.

You must be a credit union member, but joining is often simple (community credit unions accept residents of a geographic area). See our [Credit Union vs Bank comparison](/compare/credit-union-vs-bank) for help choosing between the two.

### 2. Credit Card Cash Advance

A credit card cash advance at 25–30% APR is expensive — but it's far less expensive than a 400% APR payday loan for the same short-term need. Check your card's cash advance fee (usually 3–5%) and the APR. If you can repay it in a month, a cash advance costs a fraction of what a payday loan costs.

### 3. Paycheck Advance / Earned Wage Access (EWA)

Apps like **Dave**, **Earnin**, **Brigit**, and **Payactiv** let you access wages you've already earned before payday arrives. Fees range from $0 (tip-based) to a small flat fee ($2–$5 per advance). Some employers offer EWA through payroll providers. This is effectively a 0%–low-APR bridge to your own income.

### 4. Negotiate a Payment Plan

If the expense driving the payday loan is a bill — medical, utility, rent, car payment — call and ask for a payment plan. Medical providers routinely offer extended payment plans at 0% interest. Utilities have hardship programs. Landlords may be willing to work with a good tenant. The worst they can say is no.

### 5. Community Assistance Programs

Food banks, utility assistance programs (LIHEAP), community action agencies, and local nonprofits can sometimes cover specific costs (groceries, electricity, medication) that would otherwise drive you to borrow. A social worker at a hospital or community organization can help identify programs you qualify for.

### 6. Borrow From Family or Friends

Awkward, but genuinely less damaging than a debt spiral. If someone in your network can lend $300 with no fee and a flexible repayment schedule, that costs you $0. Write up a simple repayment agreement if it helps both parties feel more comfortable.

## What to Do If You're Already in a Payday Loan Cycle

If you're rolling over a payday loan repeatedly:

1. **Stop rolling over** — each rollover is another fee with no reduction in principal.
2. **Contact a nonprofit credit counselor** — the National Foundation for Credit Counseling (NFCC) offers free or low-cost counseling. They can help you identify options and may negotiate with lenders on your behalf.
3. **Check your state's repayment plan rules** — some states require payday lenders to offer an extended repayment plan (ERP) at no extra charge if you can't repay. Ask your lender if this option exists.
4. **Consider a PAL to pay off the payday loan** — if you can get a credit union PAL, use it to eliminate the payday loan debt at a lower rate and with a multi-month repayment window.
5. **Talk to the lender** — lenders sometimes prefer negotiation to default. Ask directly if any hardship options exist.

## Key Takeaways

- **A payday loan** is a short-term loan (usually $100–$1,000) due in full on your next payday, with fees that translate to APRs of 300%–600%+
- **The debt trap** is real: 80% of payday loans are rolled over, and fees compound rapidly because each rollover extends the loan without reducing the principal
- **Legal status varies by state** — payday lending is banned or heavily restricted in about a dozen states; online lenders sometimes evade state caps via tribal or out-of-state structures
- **Credit union PALs** are the closest thing to a payday loan alternative: fast approval, limited credit check, 28% APR cap
- **Earned wage access apps** let you access money you've already earned — often at zero or minimal cost — and are the best alternative for workers with a steady paycheck
- If you're in a payday loan cycle, stop rolling over, call a nonprofit counselor, and check whether your state has a mandatory extended repayment plan
