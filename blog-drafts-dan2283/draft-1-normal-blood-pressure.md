# Normal Blood Pressure: Ranges by Age, What the Numbers Mean, and When to Worry

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 21, 2026*

Normal blood pressure is defined as a reading below **120/80 mmHg** (millimeters of mercury). That single benchmark shapes how doctors screen patients, prescribe medications, and gauge cardiovascular risk — but the complete picture is more nuanced. Age, health history, time of day, and the conditions under which you take a reading all influence what "normal" looks like for you personally.

This guide explains what the two numbers measure, how blood pressure categories work, how normal ranges shift across the lifespan, and what to do if your numbers fall outside the healthy zone.

---

## What the Two Numbers Mean

Every blood pressure reading has two parts separated by a slash:

**Systolic pressure (the top number)** measures the force your heart exerts against artery walls when it contracts and pumps blood. It's the higher of the two values.

**Diastolic pressure (the bottom number)** measures the pressure in your arteries between heartbeats, when the heart muscle relaxes and refills. It's always the lower value.

The unit — mmHg — refers to how high the pressure can push a column of mercury, a legacy of the early instruments used to measure blood pressure. Modern sphygmomanometers don't contain mercury, but the unit stuck.

### Reading a Blood Pressure Value

A reading of **120/80 mmHg** is read as "120 over 80." The systolic is 120; the diastolic is 80. Both numbers matter, and either number in an elevated range is clinically significant on its own.

---

## Blood Pressure Categories (AHA Guidelines)

The American Heart Association (AHA) defines five blood pressure categories:

| Category | Systolic | | Diastolic |
|----------|----------|-|-----------|
| **Normal** | Less than 120 | and | Less than 80 |
| **Elevated** | 120–129 | and | Less than 80 |
| **High (Stage 1)** | 130–139 | or | 80–89 |
| **High (Stage 2)** | 140 or higher | or | 90 or higher |
| **Hypertensive Crisis** | Higher than 180 | and/or | Higher than 120 |

**Normal:** Below 120/80 is the target. At this range, cardiovascular disease risk is lowest.

**Elevated:** Systolic of 120–129 with a diastolic still under 80. No medication is typically needed, but lifestyle changes are recommended to prevent progression to Stage 1 hypertension.

**Stage 1 Hypertension:** Systolic 130–139 or diastolic 80–89. Doctors often recommend lifestyle changes first; medication may be added depending on overall cardiovascular risk.

**Stage 2 Hypertension:** Systolic 140+ or diastolic 90+. Lifestyle changes plus one or more blood pressure medications are standard practice.

**Hypertensive Crisis:** Above 180 systolic and/or above 120 diastolic. Seek emergency care immediately. This can cause a stroke, heart attack, or organ damage.

## Normal Blood Pressure by Age

Blood pressure tends to rise gradually with age as arteries stiffen. What's considered normal (or acceptable with management) changes across life stages:

### Children and Adolescents (Ages 1–17)

Blood pressure norms in children are calculated relative to height percentile and age rather than fixed numbers. In general, readings should be below the 90th percentile for age, sex, and height. A pediatrician interprets children's BP readings on growth charts.

### Adults Ages 18–39

Healthy range: **under 120/80 mmHg**

In younger adults, a reading consistently around 115/75 is considered optimal. Readings in the 120–129 / below 80 range are "elevated" and a signal to improve diet, exercise, and stress management. High blood pressure in young adults warrants evaluation because it's less expected and may point to secondary causes (kidney disease, hormonal disorders).

### Adults Ages 40–59

Healthy range: **under 120/80 mmHg** (same target)

The risk of hypertension rises sharply in this age group. Regular monitoring becomes more important. Stage 1 hypertension (130–139/80–89) is common and often managed with lifestyle changes before medication is introduced.

### Adults Ages 60+

Healthy target: **under 130/80 mmHg** for most; some guidelines accept up to 140/90 for adults over 80 depending on frailty and fall risk

The risk-benefit calculation for aggressive BP lowering changes in older adults. Very low systolic readings (under 110 in the very old) can increase fall risk. Your doctor balances the cardiovascular benefit of lower BP against these risks.

| Age Group | Normal Systolic | Normal Diastolic |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| 18–39 | < 120 | < 80 |
| 40–59 | < 120 | < 80 |
| 60–79 | < 130 | < 80 |
| 80+ | < 140 (individualized) | < 80–90 |

## Factors That Affect Your Blood Pressure Reading

Blood pressure fluctuates throughout the day. Several factors can cause a temporary spike or drop that doesn't reflect your true baseline:

**White coat hypertension** — Blood pressure is often higher in a clinical setting because of anxiety. If your readings are consistently elevated at the doctor's office but normal at home, your doctor may recommend a 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitor or home readings over several days.

**Time of day** — BP is typically lowest in the early morning hours and rises as you wake and move around. It peaks in the mid-afternoon and drops at night.

**Physical activity** — Exercise temporarily raises blood pressure. A reading taken within 30 minutes of exercise will be higher than your resting baseline.

**Caffeine and alcohol** — Both can transiently elevate blood pressure.

**Full bladder** — A full bladder can raise a reading by 10–15 mmHg.

**Talking or moving during measurement** — Sit still and stay quiet during the reading.

**Incorrect cuff size** — A cuff too small for your arm gives falsely high readings; one too large gives falsely low readings.

**Body position** — Sit with your back supported, feet flat on the floor, and arm at heart level. Slouching or crossing your legs raises the reading.

## Low Blood Pressure: When Does It Become a Problem?

Low blood pressure (hypotension) is generally defined as **below 90/60 mmHg**. For many people — particularly young, fit adults — a reading of 90/60 causes no symptoms and is perfectly healthy. The problem with hypotension is not the number itself but the symptoms it may produce:

- Dizziness or lightheadedness, especially when standing up (orthostatic hypotension)
- Fainting
- Blurred vision
- Fatigue or weakness
- Nausea
- Cold or clammy skin

**Orthostatic hypotension** — a drop of 20+ mmHg systolic when you stand from seated or lying down — is common in older adults and can increase fall risk. It's often caused by dehydration, medications, or prolonged bed rest.

If you experience symptoms of low blood pressure, consult a doctor before assuming it's harmless.

## How to Measure Blood Pressure Accurately at Home

Home blood pressure monitoring is increasingly recommended for a fuller picture of your cardiovascular health. Here's how to get an accurate reading:

1. **Choose a validated monitor** — Look for devices certified by recognized bodies (Validated Blood Pressure Monitor List, American Medical Association). Upper-arm cuffs are more accurate than wrist monitors for most people.
2. **Sit quietly for 5 minutes** before taking a reading.
3. **Sit with your back supported** and both feet flat on the floor.
4. **Position your arm at heart level** — rest it on a table if possible.
5. **Use the correct cuff size** — the cuff should cover 80% of your upper arm's circumference.
6. **Don't talk or move** during the measurement.
7. **Take two readings** one minute apart; record both.
8. **Measure at the same time each day** — ideally morning before medication and evening.
9. **Bring your home log to doctor appointments** — a week's worth of readings is far more informative than a single office measurement.

## What Raises Blood Pressure Over Time

Unlike acute spikes, sustained high blood pressure (hypertension) develops over years and is driven by:

- **Diet high in sodium** — Most Americans consume 3,400 mg of sodium per day, well above the recommended 2,300 mg (and ideally 1,500 mg for those with hypertension)
- **Physical inactivity**
- **Excess body weight** — Each 10-lb of excess weight can raise systolic BP by 4–5 mmHg
- **Chronic stress**
- **Heavy alcohol consumption** — More than 1–2 drinks per day consistently raises BP
- **Tobacco use**
- **Sleep apnea** — Untreated obstructive sleep apnea is a significant driver of secondary hypertension
- **Chronic kidney disease**
- **Hormonal disorders** (thyroid dysfunction, Cushing's syndrome, primary aldosteronism)
- **Certain medications** — NSAIDs, oral contraceptives, decongestants, and some antidepressants can raise BP

## How to Lower Blood Pressure Without Medication

For Stage 1 hypertension (130–139/80–89), many doctors recommend a 3–6 month lifestyle trial before prescribing medication. Evidence-backed changes that reduce blood pressure:

| Intervention | Average Systolic Reduction |
|---|---|
| DASH diet | 8–14 mmHg |
| Sodium reduction to < 1,500 mg/day | 5–6 mmHg |
| Regular aerobic exercise (150 min/week) | 4–9 mmHg |
| Limit alcohol to ≤ 1 drink/day | 2–4 mmHg |
| Lose 10 lbs if overweight | 5–20 mmHg |
| Quit smoking | 5–10 mmHg long-term |

The **DASH diet** (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) is clinically the best-studied dietary intervention for blood pressure. It emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy, lean proteins, and limited sodium and saturated fat.

## When to See a Doctor

See your doctor if:
- Any reading is **140/90 or higher** on two separate occasions
- You experience symptoms of low blood pressure (dizziness, fainting)
- Your home readings consistently show **130–139 systolic** despite lifestyle changes
- You have a single reading above **180/120** — go to an emergency room immediately

## Key Takeaways

- **Normal blood pressure** is below **120/80 mmHg** for adults of all ages
- **Elevated** is 120–129 systolic with diastolic under 80 — lifestyle changes are the first step
- **Stage 1 hypertension** begins at 130/80; **Stage 2** at 140/90
- Blood pressure rises with age — the target for adults 60+ is generally under 130/80
- A single high reading is not a diagnosis; hypertension requires consistently elevated readings over time
- Lifestyle changes (DASH diet, exercise, sodium reduction, weight loss) can lower systolic BP by 5–14 mmHg each — enough to avoid medication for mild hypertension
- Monitor at home with a validated cuff and bring your log to your doctor
