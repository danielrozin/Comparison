# Calorie Deficit Diet: How to Calculate It, How Large a Deficit to Run, and What to Eat

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 23, 2026*

A calorie deficit occurs when you consume fewer calories than your body burns over a given period. It is the foundational mechanism behind virtually every diet that produces fat loss — regardless of whether the diet calls itself keto, Mediterranean, paleo, intermittent fasting, or anything else. The approach, the food rules, and the eating windows all vary; the calorie deficit underneath doesn't.

This guide explains how a calorie deficit works, how to calculate the right size for your goals, which foods make it easier to sustain, and the common reasons a deficit "stops working" despite apparent adherence.

---

## The Core Principle: Energy Balance

Your body weight is governed by **energy balance**: the relationship between calories in (food and drink) and calories out (all the ways your body expends energy).

**Calories in < Calories out = Calorie deficit = Weight loss**  
**Calories in > Calories out = Calorie surplus = Weight gain**  
**Calories in = Calories out = Calorie maintenance = Weight stable**

This is often called CICO (calories in, calories out). It's not a complete explanation of physiology — hormones, sleep, stress, gut health, and medication all matter — but energy balance remains the dominant variable in fat loss for the vast majority of people.

**One pound of fat ≈ 3,500 calories.** A consistent daily deficit of 500 calories creates roughly a 3,500-calorie weekly deficit, which in theory produces about 1 pound of fat loss per week. In practice, results vary, but this estimate is a useful starting point.

## How to Calculate Your Calorie Needs

### Step 1: Find Your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Your BMR is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep your heart beating, lungs breathing, and organs functioning. The most commonly used formula is the **Mifflin-St Jeor equation**:

**For men:**
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age in years) + 5

**For women:**
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age in years) − 161

**Example (35-year-old woman, 68 kg, 165 cm):**
BMR = (10 × 68) + (6.25 × 165) − (5 × 35) − 161
BMR = 680 + 1,031.25 − 175 − 161 = **1,375 calories/day**

### Step 2: Multiply by Your Activity Multiplier (TDEE)

Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is your BMR adjusted for activity:

| Activity Level | Description | Multiplier |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary | Desk job, little/no exercise | 1.2 |
| Lightly active | Light exercise 1–3 days/week | 1.375 |
| Moderately active | Moderate exercise 3–5 days/week | 1.55 |
| Very active | Hard exercise 6–7 days/week | 1.725 |
| Extra active | Physical job + hard training | 1.9 |

Continuing the example (moderately active):
TDEE = 1,375 × 1.55 = **2,131 calories/day**

This is the maintenance level — the amount needed to hold current weight.

### Step 3: Set Your Deficit

Subtract from TDEE to create a deficit:

| Goal | Daily Deficit | Weekly Loss (Theoretical) |
|---|---|---|
| Mild / sustainable | 250 calories | ~0.5 lb/week |
| Standard | 500 calories | ~1 lb/week |
| Aggressive | 750–1,000 calories | ~1.5–2 lb/week |
| Crash / unsustainable | > 1,000 calories | Often backfires |

For the example above, a 500-calorie deficit means eating **~1,631 calories/day**.

**Important floor:** Most guidelines recommend against eating below **1,200 calories/day for women** or **1,500 calories/day for men** without medical supervision. Below these floors, meeting nutritional needs becomes very difficult and metabolic adaptation accelerates.

## How Big a Deficit Should You Run?

The "right" size depends on your goals, timeline, and starting weight.

### Larger Deficits: Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Faster weight loss (visible results motivate adherence)
- Useful when a deadline exists (e.g., 8 weeks to an event)

**Cons:**
- Higher muscle loss — large deficits paired with insufficient protein accelerate lean mass loss
- Hormonal disruption — aggressive restriction suppresses leptin, raises ghrelin, and signals the body to conserve energy
- Harder to maintain — hunger, fatigue, and irritability increase with deficit size
- Metabolic adaptation — the body partially compensates by lowering TDEE

### Smaller Deficits: Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Better muscle preservation
- Lower hunger — more sustainable long-term
- Metabolic adaptation is slower

**Cons:**
- Slower progress, which can erode motivation if results feel too distant

**Best practice for most people:** A deficit of **300–500 calories/day**, paired with adequate protein (0.7–1 gram per pound of body weight), produces steady fat loss with muscle preservation and is manageable for most people over months, not just weeks.

## What to Eat on a Calorie Deficit Diet

Any foods fit within a calorie deficit, in principle. But some foods make maintaining the deficit far easier than others because of their effect on satiety, nutrient density, and volume.

### High-Satiety, Low-Calorie Foods

These foods provide maximum fullness per calorie:

**Protein sources:**
- Chicken breast (165 cal per 100g, 31g protein)
- Greek yogurt, plain (59 cal per 100g, 10g protein)
- Eggs (155 cal per 100g, 13g protein)
- Canned tuna (116 cal per 100g, 26g protein)
- Cottage cheese (98 cal per 100g, 11g protein)
- Shrimp (99 cal per 100g, 24g protein)

**Vegetables (very low calorie, high volume and fiber):**
- Broccoli (34 cal per 100g)
- Spinach (23 cal per 100g)
- Cucumber (15 cal per 100g)
- Zucchini (17 cal per 100g)
- Cabbage (25 cal per 100g)

**Fruits (moderate calorie, high fiber and water):**
- Strawberries (32 cal per 100g)
- Watermelon (30 cal per 100g)
- Apples (52 cal per 100g)
- Blueberries (57 cal per 100g)

**Fiber-rich carbs:**
- Oats (68 cal per 100g cooked)
- Lentils (116 cal per 100g cooked)
- Black beans (132 cal per 100g cooked)
- Sweet potato (86 cal per 100g cooked)

### Foods That Work Against the Deficit

These foods are calorie-dense, low in fiber and protein, and easy to overconsume:

- Ultra-processed snacks (chips, cookies, crackers)
- Caloric beverages (soda, juice, alcohol, specialty coffee drinks)
- Nut butters and oils (healthy, but 100+ calories per tablespoon)
- Cheese (110+ calories per ounce)
- Processed meats
- Fast food and fried foods

**A note on fats:** Fat is not bad, but it is calorie-dense — 9 calories per gram compared to 4 for protein or carbohydrates. Healthy fats (avocado, olive oil, nuts) have real nutritional value; they just require careful portioning on a calorie deficit.

## Why the Deficit "Stops Working"

Many people report losing weight for several weeks, then stalling without changing anything. Several mechanisms explain this:

### 1. Metabolic Adaptation

As you lose weight, your TDEE decreases — a smaller body burns fewer calories. Your maintenance level drops, so the same intake that once created a 500-calorie deficit now creates a smaller one. Solution: recalculate TDEE after every 10 lbs of weight loss and adjust intake accordingly.

### 2. Tracking Errors

Studies consistently show that people underestimate calorie intake by 20–50%. The most common culprits:
- Cooking oils (easy to pour too much)
- Sauces and dressings (often 100–200 calories per serving)
- Bites, tastes, and licks while cooking
- Not tracking weekends or "treat" days

Weighing food with a kitchen scale, rather than estimating volume, dramatically improves tracking accuracy.

### 3. Activity Adaptation

As you lose weight, exercise burns fewer calories (less mass to move). Additionally, NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — fidgeting, standing, incidental movement) often decreases unconsciously when you're eating less, as the body conserves energy.

### 4. Water Retention

Weight fluctuates by 2–4 lbs daily due to water retention, glycogen stores, and digestion. A week of no scale movement doesn't mean no fat loss — it often means fat loss is masked by water retention (hormonal shifts, high sodium, inflammation from hard exercise). Track weight as a 7–14 day moving average.

## Calorie Deficit vs. Specific Diets

| Diet | Core Mechanism | Deficit Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Keto / low-carb** | Eliminate carbs | Reduces food choices, reduces insulin → may reduce appetite |
| **Intermittent fasting** | Restrict eating window | Fewer hours to eat → often naturally lower intake |
| **Mediterranean** | Whole foods, healthy fats | High satiety per calorie |
| **Low-fat** | Reduce fat intake | Reducing calorie-dense foods |
| **Paleo** | Eliminate processed foods | Removes high-calorie processed foods |

Every successful weight-loss diet creates a calorie deficit. The dietary framework determines *how* you get there and whether you can sustain it. ## Practical Tips for Sustaining a Calorie Deficit

- **Prioritize protein** (aim for 0.7–1g per pound of body weight) — it's the most satiating macronutrient and preserves muscle during weight loss
- **Eat volume foods first** — start meals with salad, soup, or vegetables to reduce hunger before the calorie-dense portions
- **Don't drink calories** — liquid calories don't suppress hunger the same way solid food does; water, sparkling water, black coffee, and unsweetened tea are essentially free
- **Plan and track before you eat, not after** — logging food in advance prevents end-of-day overage surprises
- **Take a diet break** — 1–2 weeks at maintenance calories every 8–12 weeks gives hormones a reset and often improves adherence without ending progress
- **Sleep 7–9 hours** — sleep deprivation increases ghrelin (hunger hormone) and decreases leptin (satiety hormone), making the deficit dramatically harder to maintain

---

## Key Takeaways

- A **calorie deficit** — consuming fewer calories than you burn — is the mechanism behind all fat loss
- Calculate your TDEE, then subtract 300–500 calories for a sustainable, 0.5–1 lb/week loss rate
- **Protein intake** (0.7–1g per pound of body weight) is the most important dietary variable — it preserves muscle and keeps you fuller
- High-volume, low-calorie foods (vegetables, lean proteins, legumes) make the deficit easier to sustain
- Tracking errors and metabolic adaptation are the most common reasons a deficit "stops working" — recalculate TDEE every 10 lbs lost and weigh food instead of estimating
- Every major diet (keto, fasting, Mediterranean, paleo) works through the same mechanism: creating a calorie deficit; the difference is the method, not the physics
