# Christmas Dinner Ideas: 12 Menus That Work for Any Table Size

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | November 20, 2026*

The biggest decision in Christmas dinner is the protein — everything else is built around it. Choose that first based on guest count, skill level, and oven space, and the rest of the menu falls into place. A prime rib roast is the showstopper for 6–10 guests with experience; a roast chicken is the reliable choice for 2–4; a beef tenderloin is the fastest impressive option for 4–6. This guide covers 12 full menus — including a complete timeline for the most popular option — so you can skip directly to whatever fits your situation.

## How to Think About Christmas Dinner Structure

A Christmas dinner has three moving parts: the protein (which anchors timing), the sides (which can mostly be made ahead), and the table (which is where most of the stress comes from, not the cooking).

**Protein timing is everything.** Every other dish can be flexible — sides can wait, appetizers can stall, desserts can be served late — but the main protein has a narrow window between done and overdone. Build your entire timeline backward from the target serving time.

**Sides should require as little last-minute cooking as possible.** Gratins, casseroles, and roasted vegetables can all be partially or fully prepared ahead and finished in the oven while the protein rests. The fewer things that need the stovetop in the last 30 minutes, the less chaotic dinner service will be.

**Oven temperature conflicts are real.** If your protein roasts at 325°F but your roasted vegetables need 425°F, you can't run them simultaneously. Plan to either finish vegetables before the protein goes in, or roast them at a lower temp (they'll take longer but will still be good).

## Menu 1: Classic Prime Rib (Serves 6–10)

Prime rib is the most impressive Christmas dinner choice and less technically difficult than its reputation suggests. The key is using a meat thermometer and not guessing.

**What you need:**
- 1 bone-in prime rib roast, 3–4 bones (6–8 lbs for 6–8 people)
- Dry rub: 2 tbsp kosher salt, 1 tbsp black pepper, 1 tbsp garlic powder, 1 tbsp dried thyme, 2 tbsp olive oil
- Au jus: drippings, 1 cup red wine, 2 cups beef broth

**Day-before prep:**
Salt the roast generously (1 tsp kosher salt per pound of meat), place uncovered on a rack in the refrigerator overnight. This is called dry-brining and produces a better crust and more evenly seasoned interior.

**Christmas day:**
1. Remove roast from the refrigerator 2 hours before cooking. This is not optional — a cold roast center takes significantly longer to cook and produces more of the dreaded gray ring between the crust and the pink center.
2. Preheat oven to 500°F. Rub the roast with the oil-garlic-thyme mixture.
3. Roast at 500°F for 15 minutes. This creates the crust.
4. Reduce heat to 325°F. Roast until internal temperature reads 115°F for rare, 120°F for medium-rare. Use a probe thermometer for accuracy — a 7-lb roast at 325°F typically takes about 2–2.5 hours to reach 115°F.
5. Remove from oven; tent loosely with foil. Rest 30 minutes minimum — the internal temperature will rise to ~125°F (medium-rare) during resting.
6. While roast rests, make au jus: place roasting pan on stovetop over medium heat, add wine, scrape up browned bits, add broth, reduce by half.

On [fresh vs. frozen prime rib](/compare/fresh-vs-frozen-prime-rib): fresh from a butcher is preferable for even marbling and known age, but USDA Choice frozen prime rib (from Costco or a warehouse club) performs very well for home roasting and costs 30–40% less per pound.

**Sides that work with prime rib:**
- Yorkshire pudding (uses the drippings)
- Creamed spinach
- Roasted asparagus with lemon
- Twice-baked potatoes or a potato gratin

## Menu 2: Roast Chicken (Serves 2–4)

The underappreciated Christmas dinner for smaller gatherings. A single roast chicken costs $15–25, takes 90 minutes total, and — if spatchcocked (backbone removed, pressed flat) — comes out juicy and evenly cooked every time.

**Spatchcock roast chicken:**
1. Remove backbone with kitchen shears. Flatten breast side up.
2. Season aggressively with salt, pepper, smashed garlic under the skin, and lemon zest.
3. Roast at 425°F for 45–55 minutes on a rack over a sheet pan. Internal thigh temperature: 165°F.
4. Rest 10 minutes before carving.

**Sides:** Roasted root vegetables (carrots, parsnips, turnips), simple green salad, pan sauce made from drippings and chicken broth.

## Menu 3: Beef Tenderloin (Serves 4–6)

The most expensive but fastest high-impression protein. A 3-lb beef tenderloin (trimmed) feeds 6 people and takes 25–35 minutes in the oven.

**Method:**
- Tie at 2-inch intervals to even the thickness.
- Salt and refrigerate uncovered overnight.
- Sear all sides in an oven-safe skillet over high heat, 2 minutes per side.
- Transfer to 425°F oven for 20–25 minutes until internal temperature reaches 125°F.
- Rest 15 minutes. Serve with a red wine reduction or horseradish cream.

## Menu 4: Whole Roasted Salmon (Serves 4–6, 30 Minutes)

The fastest elegant option. A whole salmon (4–5 lbs, bone-in) roasts in 25–30 minutes and needs almost no technical skill.

1. Season cavity with salt, lemon, dill, and fennel fronds.
2. Rub outside with olive oil and salt.
3. Roast at 400°F for 25–30 minutes until flesh flakes at the thickest part.
4. Serve with a lemon-dill crème fraîche, capers, and thin-sliced cucumber on the side.

## Menu 5: Leg of Lamb (Serves 6–8)

Classic in many European Christmas traditions. A boneless leg of lamb is the easiest format to carve.

**Preparation:**
- Marinate in olive oil, garlic, rosemary, and lemon juice overnight.
- Roast at 325°F: 20 minutes per pound to 135°F internal temperature for medium.
- Serve with mint sauce, roasted potatoes in lamb fat, and a cucumber yogurt sauce (tzatziki).

---

## Menu 6: Ham (Serves 8–12)

The best choice for large gatherings where the host needs to minimize last-minute work. A fully-cooked spiral ham needs only glazing and warming — not actual cooking.

**The glaze formula:**
- ¼ cup Dijon mustard + ½ cup brown sugar + 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar + ½ tsp cloves
- Brush on ham; bake at 325°F for 15–18 minutes per pound; glaze every 30 minutes.

## Menu 7: Vegetarian Wellington (Serves 6)

A pastry-wrapped centerpiece for vegetarian tables. The traditional protein filling is replaced with a mushroom and spinach duxelles, often with a layer of lentils or roasted vegetables.

- Make mushroom duxelles (finely chopped mushrooms, shallots, garlic, thyme cooked dry in a skillet for 15 minutes).
- Roll puff pastry; spread with duxelles. Add roasted portobello mushroom layer.
- Roll tightly; egg wash; score the top.
- Bake at 425°F for 25–30 minutes until golden.

## Side Dish Ideas That Work for Any Menu

**Ahead-of-time sides** (can be fully made 1–2 days before):
- Green bean casserole (classic, holds well)
- Scalloped potatoes or potato gratin
- Cranberry sauce
- Stuffing or dressing (baked in a casserole dish, not in the bird)
- Roasted root vegetables (reheat at 400°F for 15 minutes)

**Day-of sides** (45 minutes or less):
- Roasted Brussels sprouts with bacon and balsamic glaze (30 minutes at 425°F)
- Honey-glazed carrots (20 minutes on stovetop)
- Simple Caesar or winter salad (10 minutes)
- Parker House rolls (use store-bought frozen dough; bake per package)

## Oven Management

Multiple dishes competing for oven space is the practical bottleneck of Christmas dinner. A few strategies:

1. **[Convection vs. conventional oven](/compare/convection-vs-conventional-oven):** convection circulates heat more evenly and typically reduces cooking time by 25%, which means you can run the protein at a slightly lower setting and fit more dishes simultaneously.

2. **Use a cooler as an insulated holding area.** A cooked dish wrapped in foil and placed in a dry cooler holds temperature for 1–2 hours. This frees oven space for other dishes.

3. **Stagger by rack.** Most proteins want the middle rack; casseroles and gratins can go on the bottom rack during the final 30 minutes if temperatures are compatible.

## Dessert Pairing

Christmas dinner desserts tend toward the heavy side — yule log cake, Christmas pudding, peppermint ice cream, or a simple warm apple tart. The most practical approach after a large dinner: serve dessert 45–60 minutes after the main course, offer coffee, and choose something that can be made entirely ahead (yule log, peppermint semifreddo) rather than requiring last-minute assembly.
