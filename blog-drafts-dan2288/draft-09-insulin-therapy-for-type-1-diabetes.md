# Insulin Therapy for Type 1 Diabetes: Types, Regimens, and Technology

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | August 9, 2026*

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the body's immune system destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. Without beta cells, the body produces no insulin — and without insulin, glucose can't enter cells for energy, leading to dangerously high blood sugar. Unlike Type 2 diabetes, which can sometimes be managed with lifestyle changes and oral medications alone, **Type 1 diabetes requires insulin therapy for survival**.

This guide covers the types of insulin used in Type 1 management, the delivery methods available, how regimens are structured, and the technology that makes modern management more precise.

---

## Why Insulin Replacement Must Be Comprehensive in Type 1

In a person without diabetes, the pancreas does two things:

1. **Releases a slow, constant stream of insulin throughout the day** — called basal insulin — to manage the liver's glucose output between meals and during sleep
2. **Releases a larger burst of insulin when food is eaten** — called a bolus — to handle the glucose spike from the meal

Type 1 diabetes eliminates both functions. Effective insulin therapy must therefore replace both:
- **Basal coverage:** Long-acting insulin to maintain stable blood glucose between meals
- **Bolus coverage:** Rapid-acting insulin before each meal or snack to manage post-meal spikes

This two-component approach is called **basal-bolus therapy**, and it's the standard of care for Type 1 diabetes.

## Types of Insulin Used in Type 1 Diabetes

### Rapid-Acting Insulin (Bolus)

Rapid-acting insulins are taken just before meals to handle the glucose rise from eating. They begin working quickly and clear the system within a few hours.

| Name | Brand(s) | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|------|---------|-------|------|---------|
| Insulin aspart | NovoLog, Fiasp | 10–15 min | 1–2 hrs | 3–5 hrs |
| Insulin lispro | Humalog, Lyumjev | 10–15 min | 1–2 hrs | 3–5 hrs |
| Insulin glulisine | Apidra | 10–15 min | 1–2 hrs | 3–5 hrs |

The "faster" versions (Fiasp, Lyumjev) were developed to reduce the gap between injection and food — relevant for people who can't predict meal timing precisely.

### Long-Acting Insulin (Basal)

Long-acting insulins provide steady background coverage without peaks, mimicking the pancreas's constant basal secretion.

| Name | Brand(s) | Onset | Peak | Duration |
|------|---------|-------|------|---------|
| Insulin glargine | Lantus, Basaglar, Toujeo | 2–4 hrs | None (flat) | 20–24 hrs (Lantus/Basaglar); up to 36 hrs (Toujeo) |
| Insulin detemir | Levemir | 1–3 hrs | Slight | 18–24 hrs |
| Insulin degludec | Tresiba | 1–9 hrs | None (flat) | 42+ hrs |

**Tresiba (degludec)** has the flattest, longest profile of any basal insulin available, which gives it more flexibility in injection timing and produces fewer nocturnal hypoglycemia events for many patients.

### Intermediate-Acting Insulin (NPH)

NPH (Neutral Protamine Hagedorn) is an older, less expensive insulin that provides intermediate coverage. It peaks, which makes it less predictable than modern basal insulins. NPH is still used in some cost-sensitive situations or in specific regimen combinations, but modern basal insulins have largely replaced it as first choice.

## Insulin Delivery Methods

### Multiple Daily Injections (MDI)

MDI is the standard delivery approach: one or two injections of long-acting basal insulin per day, plus a rapid-acting injection before each meal. Using an insulin pen (a device that holds a cartridge of insulin and allows precise dose selection in units) makes this more convenient than syringes.

**Advantages:** Simpler to start, no external device to wear, lower cost, no technical failures  
**Disadvantages:** Requires 4–6+ injections per day; less flexibility for variable meal timing; doesn't adapt to changing insulin needs in real time

### Insulin Pumps (Continuous Subcutaneous Insulin Infusion — CSII)

An insulin pump is a small device (approximately the size of a pager) worn on the body that delivers rapid-acting insulin continuously throughout the day via a small cannula inserted under the skin. The basal rate can be programmed to vary by hour; meal boluses are delivered on demand via the pump's controls.

**Advantages:** Eliminates long-acting insulin injections; allows precise fine-tuning of basal rates throughout the day; easier to manage exercise, travel, and variable schedules; better suited for people with highly variable insulin needs  
**Disadvantages:** Wearable technology that requires insertion site maintenance every 2–3 days; pump failure can cause diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) if undetected; higher upfront cost; some patients prefer not to wear a device continuously

**Common insulin pump brands:** Tandem (t:slim X2), Medtronic (670G, 770G, 780G), Omnipod (tubeless patch pump)

### Closed-Loop Systems ("Artificial Pancreas")

The most advanced available option: a continuous glucose monitor (CGM) connected to an insulin pump via an algorithm that automatically adjusts basal insulin delivery based on real-time glucose readings.

**Fully available closed-loop systems (2026):**
- **Tandem Control-IQ:** FDA-cleared; uses Dexcom G6 or G7 CGM; automatically adjusts basal rates and delivers correction boluses
- **Medtronic MiniMed 780G:** Automates basal and delivers automated correction boluses; updates every 5 minutes
- **Omnipod 5:** Tubeless patch pump; pairs with Dexcom G6; uses SmartAdjust algorithm

These systems have been shown in clinical trials to significantly reduce time spent in hypoglycemia and improve time-in-range (the percent of time blood glucose is between 70–180 mg/dL) compared to MDI or open-loop pumps.

## Common Insulin Regimens for Type 1

### Standard Basal-Bolus (MDI)

- **Basal:** Long-acting insulin (Lantus, Tresiba) once or twice daily
- **Bolus:** Rapid-acting insulin before each meal, dosed based on carbohydrate content and current blood glucose
- **Correction boluses:** Additional rapid-acting insulin when blood glucose is above target, calculated using an insulin sensitivity factor (how many mg/dL one unit of insulin lowers your glucose)

### Pump-Based Basal-Bolus

- **Basal:** Continuous rapid-acting insulin delivery via pump, programmed in different rates for different times of day
- **Bolus:** On-demand bolus at mealtime using the pump's bolus calculator
- **Automated correction:** Closed-loop systems handle this automatically

### Pre-Mixed Insulin (Rarely Used in Type 1)

Pre-mixed insulins combine basal and bolus coverage in a single injection. They are more common in Type 2 diabetes management. In Type 1, they're largely avoided because the fixed ratio doesn't accommodate the highly variable meal and activity patterns typical of people with Type 1.

## Key Concepts in Insulin Dosing

### Insulin-to-Carbohydrate Ratio (ICR)

Your ICR tells you how many grams of carbohydrate one unit of insulin will cover. If your ratio is 1:15, you take 1 unit of insulin for every 15 grams of carbohydrates eaten.

ICR varies by time of day for most people (morning insulin resistance typically means a lower ratio — more insulin per gram) and is established through pattern analysis of glucose readings.

### Insulin Sensitivity Factor (ISF) / Correction Factor

Your ISF tells you how much one unit of insulin will lower your blood glucose. If your ISF is 50, one unit of insulin will lower your blood glucose by approximately 50 mg/dL. This is used to calculate correction boluses when blood glucose is above target.

### Time-in-Range (TIR)

TIR is the percentage of time blood glucose is between 70–180 mg/dL over a given period, typically reported over 14 days. Current guidelines target at least 70% TIR for most adults with Type 1 diabetes. Modern CGM devices provide this metric directly.

---

## Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGM)

CGM is now considered standard of care alongside insulin therapy in Type 1 diabetes. A CGM is a small sensor worn on the arm, abdomen, or back of the upper arm that measures glucose in interstitial fluid every 1–5 minutes, displaying readings on a receiver or smartphone.

**Key advantages of CGM over fingerstick testing:**
- Real-time trends (is glucose rising, falling, or stable?) rather than point-in-time readings
- Alerts for low and high glucose
- Glucose history and time-in-range data
- Essential input for closed-loop systems

**Widely used CGM devices:**
- **Dexcom G7** (sensor life: 10 days; real-time display to phone/watch)
- **Libre 3** (FreeStyle; 14-day sensor; continuous real-time readings)
- **Medtronic Guardian 4** (required for Medtronic closed-loop systems)

## Managing Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)

Insulin therapy carries a risk of hypoglycemia — blood glucose below 70 mg/dL. Symptoms include shakiness, sweating, confusion, and (in severe cases) unconsciousness.

**Standard treatment for mild-moderate hypoglycemia (the "15-15 rule"):**
1. Take 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates (4 oz orange juice, 3–4 glucose tablets, regular (not diet) soda)
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Recheck blood glucose
4. Repeat if still below 70 mg/dL
5. Once above 70 mg/dL, eat a small snack if the next meal is more than an hour away

**Severe hypoglycemia** (unconscious or unable to swallow) requires glucagon. Both nasal glucagon (Baqsimi) and auto-injector glucagon (Gvoke) are available and don't require the patient to mix or draw a syringe.

## Quick Summary

- Type 1 diabetes requires lifelong insulin therapy; basal-bolus coverage replaces both the pancreas's constant background secretion and mealtime insulin bursts
- MDI (multiple daily injections) is the standard; insulin pumps offer more precision; closed-loop systems (automated insulin delivery) further improve time-in-range
- Long-acting basals (Tresiba, Lantus) + rapid-acting bolus (NovoLog, Humalog) is the most common MDI regimen
- Continuous glucose monitoring (Dexcom G7, Libre 3) is now standard of care and essential for closed-loop systems
- The 15-15 rule handles mild-moderate hypoglycemia; glucagon (Baqsimi, Gvoke) is needed for severe episodes
