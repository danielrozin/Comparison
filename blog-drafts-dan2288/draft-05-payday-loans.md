# Payday Loans: How They Work, What They Cost, and Better Alternatives

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | August 5, 2026*

A payday loan is a short-term, high-interest loan designed to be repaid when you receive your next paycheck — typically within two to four weeks. They're widely available, require minimal credit qualification, and deliver cash quickly. They're also among the most expensive forms of consumer credit available, with annual percentage rates (APRs) that routinely exceed 300% and sometimes surpass 600%.

Understanding how payday loans work — in exact dollar terms, not just APR figures — is the most effective way to evaluate whether one is worth the cost.

---

## How Payday Loans Work

### The Basic Transaction

You borrow a small amount — typically $100 to $1,000, with the average payday loan around $375. The lender gives you cash or deposits it into your bank account. In return, you either:
- Write a post-dated check for the loan amount plus fees, or
- Authorize the lender to electronically debit your bank account on the due date

When your next paycheck arrives, the lender cashes the check or withdraws the funds.

### The Fee Structure

Payday loan fees are typically expressed as a flat fee per $100 borrowed. The most common structure: **$15–$30 per $100 borrowed**.

| Loan Amount | Fee ($15/100) | Fee ($30/100) | Total Repayment |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|-----------------|
| $100 | $15 | $30 | $115–$130 |
| $300 | $45 | $90 | $345–$390 |
| $500 | $75 | $150 | $575–$650 |
| $1,000 | $150 | $300 | $1,150–$1,300 |

### What That Fee Means in APR Terms

A $15 fee on a $100 two-week loan converts to an APR of approximately **391%**. Here's how:
- Fee: $15 for 14 days
- Daily rate: $15 ÷ 14 = $1.07/day
- Annual rate: $1.07 × 365 = $391.07

Most state disclosures are required to show the APR, but the framing of payday loans as "$15 per $100" rather than "391% APR" is intentional — the flat fee is psychologically easier to accept than a three-digit percentage.

---

## The Rollover Trap

The most dangerous feature of payday loans isn't the single-transaction cost — it's rollover fees.

When a borrower can't repay the full amount on the due date, the lender offers to "roll over" the loan: pay only the fee, and extend the loan for another two weeks. Each rollover costs another full fee on the original principal.

**Example:**
- You borrow $300 with a $45 fee (due in two weeks)
- You can't repay $345, so you pay $45 to roll over for another two weeks
- After four rollovers (2 months), you've paid $180 in fees and still owe the original $300
- Total cost if you repay after four rollovers: $480 for a $300 loan

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) found that more than **80% of payday loans are rolled over or renewed within two weeks** — meaning the product is more often used as revolving debt than as the one-time bridge it's marketed as.

## State Regulations on Payday Loans

State laws vary dramatically:

| Category | States |
|----------|--------|
| No state-level cap (federal law only) | Varies year to year; several states with weak or no APR limits |
| Rate caps of 36% or less (effectively prohibit payday loans) | Colorado, Illinois, Montana, Nebraska, New Hampshire, Oregon, South Dakota, and others |
| Allows payday loans with varying regulations | Most Southern and Midwestern states |
| Banned entirely | 17+ states as of 2026 |

The 36% APR cap is the threshold below which payday lending is not economically viable for lenders. States that have adopted this cap have effectively ended traditional payday lending within their borders.

If you're in a state that bans payday loans, online lenders may attempt to operate under tribal sovereignty or out-of-state charters to evade state law. These "tribal loans" are often in legal gray areas and should be approached with particular caution.

## 7 Alternatives to Payday Loans

### 1. Credit Union Payday Alternative Loans (PALs)

Federal credit unions offer **Payday Alternative Loans (PALs)** regulated by the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA). Terms:
- Amounts: $200–$2,000
- Term: 1–12 months
- Maximum APR: 28%
- Application fee: Maximum $20

This is the most direct payday loan alternative — same purpose, fraction of the cost. You must be a member of the credit union for at least one month to qualify.

### 2. Personal Loans from Online Lenders

Online lenders like Upstart, LendingClub, and Avant offer personal loans for borrowers with fair credit (580+ score). Rates are much higher than prime borrowers get (15–36% APR), but dramatically lower than payday loan rates (300–400% APR).

For a $500 loan at 36% APR over 12 months, total interest paid: approximately $110. Compare to a $500 payday loan rolled over twice: $300+ in fees.

### 3. Cash Advance Apps

Apps like Earnin, Dave, Brigit, and MoneyLion offer small cash advances (typically $20–$500) against your upcoming paycheck with no mandatory fees. Most use a "tip" model or charge a small subscription fee ($1–$10/month) rather than a per-advance charge.

For small amounts under $200, these apps are almost always cheaper than payday loans. The trade-off: you must have regular direct deposits to qualify, and advances are limited to amounts already earned but not yet paid.

### 4. Negotiating with Creditors Directly

If a payday loan would be used to pay a bill that's overdue, contact the creditor first. Many utilities, medical providers, and landlords have hardship programs or will allow payment plans that are more affordable than a payday loan.

### 5. 401(k) or IRA Loan or Withdrawal

A 401(k) loan allows you to borrow from your own retirement savings, typically at the prime rate + 1% (far below payday loan rates), with repayment through payroll deduction. Drawbacks: not all employers allow loans, it can disrupt your retirement savings growth, and if you leave your job, the loan may come due immediately.

A hardship withdrawal (for immediate financial need) avoids repayment but triggers taxes and a 10% early withdrawal penalty if you're under 59½.

### 6. Employer-Based Emergency Funds

Some employers offer emergency loan programs or paycheck advances to employees facing financial hardship. These are interest-free or very low interest and repaid through payroll deductions. Ask your HR department about employee assistance programs.

### 7. Nonprofit and Community Assistance

If the underlying need is a specific expense (utility bill, medical cost, car repair), local nonprofits, community action agencies, and religious organizations often provide grants or low-cost loans for these specific purposes. 211.org is the national directory for finding local assistance programs.

## What to Do If You Already Have a Payday Loan

If you're trapped in a payday loan cycle (rolling over repeatedly), your options:

1. **Stop the rollover:** Pay what you can, even a partial payment, to reduce the principal
2. **Request an extended payment plan:** Many states require lenders to offer an installment repayment option if you ask before the due date; this is required by law in some jurisdictions
3. **Contact a nonprofit credit counselor:** NFCC (nfcc.org) member agencies offer free or low-cost counseling and may help negotiate with lenders
4. **Revoke ACH authorization:** If you've authorized electronic withdrawals, you have the right to revoke this authorization with your bank — this prevents the lender from draining your account, though it doesn't eliminate the debt

---

## Quick Summary

- A typical payday loan charges $15–$30 per $100 borrowed, equivalent to an APR of 300–400%
- The rollover trap — paying only the fee to extend — can turn a $300 two-week loan into $480+ over two months
- Credit union PALs (Payday Alternative Loans) at 28% APR are the closest regulated alternative
- Cash advance apps like Earnin and Dave offer small advances at far lower effective cost for regular direct deposit users
- If already in a loan cycle, request an extended payment plan or contact a nonprofit credit counselor
