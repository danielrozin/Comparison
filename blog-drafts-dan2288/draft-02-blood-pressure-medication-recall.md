# Blood Pressure Medication Recall: How to Check and What to Do

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | August 2, 2026*

Blood pressure medications are among the most frequently recalled drug categories in the United States, primarily due to contamination with nitrosamine compounds — a class of chemicals classified as probable human carcinogens. If you take a blood pressure medication, knowing how to quickly check for recalls and what steps to take is genuinely important for your health.

This guide explains why blood pressure drug recalls happen, how to verify whether your specific medication is affected, and the exact steps to take if it is.

---

## Why Blood Pressure Medications Get Recalled So Often

The most common reason for recalls in this category is **nitrosamine contamination**. Nitrosamines (including NDMA, NDEA, and NMBA) are chemical byproducts that can form during the manufacturing process under certain conditions. They've been detected in:

- Valsartan (most notably in 2018–2019)
- Losartan
- Irbesartan
- Metformin (a diabetes drug with some overlap in patients)
- Ranitidine (Zantac, a heartburn medication)

The FDA classifies nitrosamine contamination as a potential long-term cancer risk at elevated exposure levels — not an immediate danger requiring emergency action. This is why recalls are often described as precautionary: the risk from stopping your blood pressure medication abruptly is often greater than the contamination risk from continuing briefly while you arrange a replacement.

**Other recall reasons include:**
- Incorrect dosage strength (the tablet contains more or less of the active ingredient than labeled)
- Contamination with foreign particles
- Packaging or labeling errors that could lead to dosing mistakes
- Failed sterility tests

## How to Check If Your Blood Pressure Medication Is Recalled

### Method 1: FDA MedWatch Recall Database (Most Reliable)

The FDA maintains a searchable database of all drug recalls at **fda.gov/safety/recalls-market-withdrawals-safety-alerts**.

Steps:
1. Go to fda.gov and search "recalls" or navigate to the Safety section
2. Filter by product type: Drugs
3. Search for your medication name (generic or brand)
4. Review results for your manufacturer and lot number

**Lot number location:** The lot number is printed on your medication bottle or blister pack, usually on the side or bottom label, following the letters "LOT" or "Lot #."

### Method 2: FDA Drug Recall List

The FDA publishes a running list of current drug recalls at **accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/ires/**. This database is more comprehensive and searchable by manufacturer, which is useful if you know your drug was made by a specific company.

### Method 3: Your Pharmacist (Fastest for Confirmation)

Call the pharmacy where you filled your prescription. Pharmacies receive direct notification from manufacturers and wholesalers when their inventory is recalled and typically pull affected medications immediately. Give them your:
- Medication name
- Dose (e.g., 40mg, 80mg)
- Lot number from the bottle

Your pharmacist can confirm in minutes whether your specific bottle is from a recalled batch.

### Method 4: Manufacturer Recall Website

Many manufacturers set up dedicated recall pages during major recalls. During the 2018–2019 valsartan recall, for example, manufacturers like Teva, Mylan, and Aurobindo all maintained searchable tools where patients could enter their lot number to check.

## What to Do If Your Medication Is Recalled

### Step 1: Don't Stop Your Medication Without Talking to Your Doctor

This is the most important instruction. Stopping a blood pressure medication suddenly can cause **rebound hypertension** — a rapid rise in blood pressure that increases the risk of stroke, heart attack, and other cardiovascular events. For most patients, this risk is higher than the recall risk itself.

Call your doctor before making any changes. Most recalls are classified as Class II (may cause temporary or reversible adverse health consequences) or Class III (unlikely to cause harm) — not Class I emergencies requiring you to stop the drug immediately.

### Step 2: Return the Recalled Medication to Your Pharmacy

Your pharmacy will provide a refund or replacement at no cost for recalled medications. You do not need to contact the manufacturer directly. The pharmacy handles the return logistics.

Bring:
- The recalled bottle (don't discard it — the lot number is needed for the refund)
- Your original prescription or pharmacy receipt (helpful but not always required)

### Step 3: Get a Replacement Prescription

Your doctor will prescribe an alternative. For the most common recall scenario (a specific manufacturer's version of a generic drug), this is often straightforward — your doctor simply specifies a different manufacturer, or switches you to a different medication in the same drug class.

**Common alternatives by drug class:**

| If your recalled drug is... | Alternatives your doctor may consider |
|-----------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| An ARB (valsartan, losartan, irbesartan) | A different ARB from a non-recalled manufacturer, or an ACE inhibitor like lisinopril |
| An ACE inhibitor (lisinopril, enalapril) | Another ACE inhibitor, or an ARB |
| A beta-blocker (metoprolol, atenolol) | Another beta-blocker or calcium channel blocker |
| A diuretic (hydrochlorothiazide) | Chlorthalidone or another thiazide diuretic |

### Step 4: Report If You Experience Side Effects

If you believe you've experienced adverse health effects related to a recalled medication, report it to the FDA's MedWatch program at fda.gov/safety/medwatch. This is voluntary for patients but helps the FDA track outcomes associated with contaminated batches.

## Major Blood Pressure Drug Recalls: Recent History

| Year | Drug | Reason | Affected Manufacturers |
|------|------|--------|----------------------|
| 2018–2019 | Valsartan | NDMA/NDEA contamination | Multiple generic manufacturers |
| 2019 | Losartan | NMBA contamination | Sandoz, Sun Pharma, others |
| 2019 | Irbesartan | NDEA contamination | Aurobindo, ScieGen |
| 2020–2021 | Various generics | Ongoing nitrosamine testing requirements | Industry-wide |
| 2022–ongoing | Ongoing recalls | Failed dissolution tests, dosage errors, new nitrosamine discoveries | Various |

The FDA now requires manufacturers to test for nitrosamines proactively, which has increased the frequency of voluntary recalls but also reduced the amount of contaminated medication reaching patients.

## How to Stay Ahead of Future Recalls

**Sign up for FDA recall alerts:** FDA offers email subscriptions for drug safety alerts at fda.gov/safety/recalls-market-withdrawals-safety-alerts. You can filter by product type to receive alerts specifically for drugs.

**Ask your pharmacist about your manufacturer:** Generic drugs from different manufacturers can vary in quality. You can ask your pharmacy which manufacturer supplies the version they stock and look up that manufacturer's recall history.

**Annual medication review:** At least once a year, ask your doctor to review all your blood pressure medications, confirm the doses are still appropriate, and check whether any alternatives exist that might be better options for your situation.

---

## Quick Summary

- Blood pressure medication recalls are most commonly caused by nitrosamine contamination — a potential carcinogen, not an immediate emergency
- Check for recalls using the FDA database (fda.gov), your pharmacist, or the manufacturer's recall page
- **Do not stop your medication without speaking to your doctor** — rebound hypertension is a real risk
- Return recalled medication to your pharmacy for a refund or replacement at no cost
- Sign up for FDA email alerts to stay current on future recalls
