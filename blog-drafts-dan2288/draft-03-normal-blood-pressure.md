# Normal Blood Pressure by Age: What the Numbers Mean

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | August 3, 2026*

A normal blood pressure reading for adults is **less than 120/80 mmHg**. That's the current guideline from the American Heart Association (AHA) and the American College of Cardiology (ACC) — and it's a tighter standard than what was considered normal before the 2017 guidelines update. Understanding what your reading actually means, how it changes with age, and what factors drive it higher or lower is the first step toward managing it effectively.

---

## How to Read a Blood Pressure Number

Blood pressure is expressed as two numbers separated by a slash: for example, **118/76 mmHg**.

- **Systolic pressure (top number):** The pressure in your arteries when your heart beats and pumps blood. This is the number that rises most as you age and is the primary driver of cardiovascular risk in adults over 50.
- **Diastolic pressure (bottom number):** The pressure in your arteries between heartbeats, when your heart is at rest.
- **mmHg:** Millimeters of mercury — the unit of measurement for blood pressure.

Both numbers matter, but a high systolic reading (140 or above) is a significant risk factor for stroke and heart disease even when the diastolic is normal.

---

## Blood Pressure Categories (AHA 2026 Guidelines)

| Category | Systolic | | Diastolic |
|----------|----------|---|-----------|
| Normal | Less than 120 | and | Less than 80 |
| Elevated | 120–129 | and | Less than 80 |
| High Blood Pressure Stage 1 | 130–139 | or | 80–89 |
| High Blood Pressure Stage 2 | 140 or higher | or | 90 or higher |
| Hypertensive Crisis | Higher than 180 | and/or | Higher than 120 |

**Note:** A single elevated reading doesn't diagnose hypertension. Blood pressure fluctuates throughout the day. Diagnosis requires elevated readings on two or more separate occasions.

## Normal Blood Pressure by Age

Blood pressure naturally tends to increase with age as arteries become stiffer. The following ranges reflect what's typical (not necessarily optimal) at each life stage:

### Children (3–12 years)
Normal blood pressure in children varies significantly by age, sex, and height. General ranges:
- Ages 3–6: ~90/55 mmHg
- Ages 7–10: ~95/60 mmHg
- Ages 11–12: ~100/65 mmHg

Pediatric blood pressure is evaluated against percentile charts — a reading that's "high" for a small child may be normal for a larger one.

### Teenagers (13–18 years)
- Typical normal: 105–120/60–75 mmHg
- High blood pressure in teenagers is increasingly common, often linked to obesity, high salt intake, and sedentary behavior

### Adults (19–40 years)
- **Ideal:** Less than 120/80 mmHg
- This is the age range where lifestyle interventions have the greatest long-term impact
- Many people in their 20s and 30s have elevated readings (120–129 systolic) without being aware of it

### Middle-Aged Adults (41–60 years)
- **Normal:** Less than 120/80 mmHg remains the target
- **Common finding:** Isolated systolic hypertension (systolic rises, diastolic stays normal) becomes more prevalent
- The 2017 guideline change reclassified 130–139/80–89 from "pre-hypertension" to Stage 1 hypertension, which means more people in this age group are now in the treatable category

### Older Adults (60+)
- Blood pressure management in older adults is more nuanced
- Very aggressive lowering (below 120 systolic) may increase fall risk in frail older patients
- The SPRINT trial found that targeting systolic below 120 reduced cardiovascular events but increased kidney injury and fainting events in some patients
- Most guidelines now recommend individualized targets for adults over 75, factoring in frailty, cognitive status, and polypharmacy

## What Causes Blood Pressure to Change?

### Factors That Raise Blood Pressure
- **High sodium intake:** The average American consumes about 3,400 mg of sodium per day; the AHA recommends under 2,300 mg (ideal: 1,500 mg) for people with hypertension
- **Obesity:** Each 10 kg of excess weight raises systolic blood pressure by roughly 5 mmHg
- **Physical inactivity:** Sedentary people have a 30–50% higher risk of hypertension than active people
- **Alcohol:** More than one drink per day (women) or two (men) raises blood pressure
- **Stress:** Causes short-term spikes; chronic stress contributes to sustained elevation through hormonal mechanisms
- **Smoking:** Raises blood pressure immediately after each cigarette; long-term smoking damages arteries
- **Sleep apnea:** Causes repeated oxygen drops during sleep that trigger blood pressure spikes, often leading to sustained hypertension
- **Caffeine:** Causes a short-term spike of 5–10 mmHg in non-habitual coffee drinkers; habitual coffee drinkers show less effect

### Factors That Lower Blood Pressure
- **Exercise:** Regular aerobic exercise reduces systolic pressure by an average of 5–8 mmHg
- **DASH diet:** Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension — emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy while limiting sodium, saturated fat, and red meat
- **Weight loss:** Losing 5% of body weight can reduce systolic pressure by 5 mmHg or more
- **Potassium:** Helps counteract sodium's blood pressure effects; found in bananas, sweet potatoes, beans, and leafy greens
- **Reducing sodium:** Cutting 1,000 mg of sodium per day lowers systolic pressure by an average of 5 mmHg in hypertensive individuals

## White Coat Hypertension vs. Masked Hypertension

**White coat hypertension:** Blood pressure is elevated at medical appointments but normal at home. Estimated to affect 15–30% of people with apparent hypertension. Home monitoring or 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring is used to distinguish this from true hypertension.

**Masked hypertension:** The opposite — blood pressure appears normal at the doctor's office but is actually elevated at home or during daily activities. This is harder to detect and may be more dangerous because it often goes untreated. People who smoke, drink heavily, or have diabetes are at higher risk.

## How to Take an Accurate Blood Pressure Reading at Home

Technique significantly affects the accuracy of home readings:

1. **Sit quietly for 5 minutes** before measuring — activity elevates readings temporarily
2. **Use the right cuff size** — a cuff that's too small gives falsely high readings
3. **Sit correctly:** Back supported, feet flat on the floor, arm at heart level
4. **Don't talk during the reading**
5. **Avoid caffeine, exercise, and smoking** for 30 minutes before measuring
6. **Take two or three readings** one to two minutes apart and record the average
7. **Measure at the same time of day** — blood pressure is typically lowest in the morning and higher in the late afternoon

---

## When to See a Doctor

**See a doctor if:**
- Home readings consistently show 130/80 mmHg or higher on multiple occasions
- You have readings above 140/90 at the doctor's office
- You experience symptoms alongside elevated readings: severe headache, vision changes, chest pain, or difficulty breathing

**Emergency (call 911 or go to the ER if):**
- Systolic reading above 180 and/or diastolic above 120 — this is a hypertensive crisis
- Any of the above symptoms combined with very high readings

---

## Quick Summary

- Normal blood pressure for adults is less than 120/80 mmHg
- 130/80–139/89 is Stage 1 hypertension (previously called "pre-hypertension") under current AHA guidelines
- Blood pressure naturally increases with age; targets for older adults are individualized
- Lifestyle changes (reduced sodium, exercise, weight loss) can lower systolic pressure by 5–10 mmHg each
- Take home readings consistently using proper technique; single readings at the doctor's office can be misleading
