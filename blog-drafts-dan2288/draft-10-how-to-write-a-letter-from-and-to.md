# How to Write a Letter (From and To Address Format)

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | August 10, 2026*

Writing a letter with the correct "from" and "to" address format matters whether you're sending a formal business letter, a professional inquiry, or an envelope through the mail. The standard format is taught less commonly now that email dominates communication — which means when a formal letter is required (a job application, a legal notice, a complaint, a reference request), many people aren't sure where to put the sender's address, the recipient's address, the date, or how to open and close properly.

This guide covers the complete format for a formal letter, with templates you can adapt directly.

---

## The Standard Business Letter Format (Block Format)

The most widely accepted format in the United States is **block format**: everything is left-aligned, paragraphs are not indented, and a blank line separates each section. This is the format used in professional correspondence.

### Complete Structure (in order, top to bottom):

```
[Your Name]
[Your Street Address]
[City, State ZIP Code]
[Your Email] (optional in formal letters)
[Your Phone Number] (optional)

[Date]

[Recipient's Full Name]
[Recipient's Title]
[Company or Organization Name]
[Recipient's Street Address]
[City, State ZIP Code]

Dear [Recipient's Name]:

[Opening paragraph — state the purpose of your letter]

[Body paragraph(s) — provide details, context, or argument]

[Closing paragraph — state what action you're requesting or what happens next]

Sincerely,

[Your handwritten signature (if printing and mailing)]

[Your typed name]
[Your title (if applicable)]
```

---

## The "From" Address (Sender's Address)

In a formal letter, the sender's address appears in the **upper left corner** (in block format) or the **upper right corner** (in older modified block format). Block format — upper left — is now standard.

**What to include in the from address:**
- Your full name
- Your street address or PO Box
- City, state, and ZIP code
- Your email address (optional, but useful for business correspondence)

**What to omit:**
- Your name on the envelope is redundant with the signature at the bottom — include it in the letterhead-style header only if you want the recipient to see it before opening
- Do not include a country unless the letter is going internationally

**Example:**
```
Sarah Chen
4421 Maple Street
Austin, TX 78701
sarah.chen@email.com
```

---

## The "To" Address (Recipient's Address)

The recipient's address appears **below the date**, on the left side of the page, regardless of whether you use block or modified block format.

**What to include:**
- The recipient's full name
- Their professional title (if applicable)
- The name of their organization (if applicable)
- Their complete mailing address

**Name courtesy titles:**
- Use "Mr." for men, "Ms." for women (unless you know they prefer "Mrs." or "Miss")
- For professionals with formal titles, use their title: "Dr.," "Professor," "The Honorable"
- When you don't know the recipient's name: use "Hiring Manager" or the department name rather than "To Whom It May Concern" (the latter reads as generic and outdated)

**Example:**
```
Marcus Williams
Director of Human Resources
Meridian Solutions Inc.
1200 Commerce Drive, Suite 400
Chicago, IL 60601
```

---

## The Date

The date appears **between the sender's address and the recipient's address**, left-aligned.

**Format:** Write the full month name, day, and four-digit year.

✅ Correct: `August 10, 2026`  
❌ Avoid: `8/10/26` (ambiguous; different countries interpret this differently) or `10 Aug 26` (informal)

**International letters:** If writing to or from outside the United States, be aware that `8/10/26` could be read as October 8 in countries that use day/month/year format. Spelling out the month eliminates ambiguity.

---

## The Salutation

The salutation is the greeting that opens the letter body. It appears directly below the recipient's address, with a blank line separating them.

**Standard formal salutation:** `Dear [Name]:`

Note the **colon** after the name — in formal business letters, a colon is correct. A comma is appropriate for personal or semi-formal letters.

| Situation | Salutation |
|-----------|-----------|
| You know the recipient's full name | `Dear Ms. Chen:` |
| You know the recipient's first name (familiar business context) | `Dear Sarah:` |
| You don't know the recipient's name but know their title | `Dear Hiring Manager:` or `Dear Human Resources Director:` |
| You know the company but not the department | `Dear [Company Name] Team:` |
| Formal legal letter with unknown recipient | `Dear Sir or Madam:` |
| Old-style general address | `To Whom It May Concern:` (acceptable but dated) |

---

## The Body Paragraphs

A formal letter should be direct. Readers of business correspondence appreciate brevity. Standard structure:

**Opening paragraph (1–3 sentences):**
State exactly why you are writing. Don't build up to the point — lead with it.

❌ Weak: "I hope this letter finds you well. My name is David Park, and I have been a customer of yours for several years. I am writing today regarding a matter that has been concerning me."

✅ Strong: "I am writing to dispute a $340 charge that appeared on my account statement dated July 15, 2026 (Account #XXXX-4892). I did not authorize this charge and am requesting an investigation and reversal."

**Body paragraphs (as many as needed):**
Provide context, evidence, or argument. Each paragraph should make one point. Use bullet points or numbered lists if you're presenting multiple pieces of information — lists are easier to read than long paragraphs in formal correspondence.

**Closing paragraph (1–3 sentences):**
State clearly what action you're requesting (or what you'll do next), and provide a deadline if one is relevant. End with a sentence that leaves the door open for follow-up.

---

## The Closing and Signature

**Standard closings by formality level:**

| Closing | Formality Level |
|---------|----------------|
| Sincerely, | Standard formal |
| Yours truly, | Formal (slightly warmer) |
| Best regards, | Business formal (widely used) |
| Regards, | Standard business |
| Thank you, | Appropriate when expressing gratitude |
| Respectfully, | Used when writing to government officials or judges |
| Yours sincerely, | British English; formal |

A comma follows the closing.

**Between the closing and your typed name:** Leave four blank lines if you're printing the letter for physical signature (this is where your handwritten signature goes). In electronic letters sent as PDFs, you may include a digital signature image or simply use two blank lines.

## Envelope Formatting (From and To on an Envelope)

When mailing a physical letter, the envelope has its own from/to format:

**Return address (FROM):**
Upper left corner of the envelope:
```
Sarah Chen
4421 Maple Street
Austin, TX 78701
```

**Recipient address (TO):**
Center of the envelope, slightly below center:
```
Marcus Williams
Meridian Solutions Inc.
1200 Commerce Drive, Suite 400
Chicago, IL 60601
```

**Postage:** Upper right corner. For standard first-class mail within the US: one Forever Stamp (current value: $0.73 as of mid-2026). Letters over 1 oz require additional postage.

**Key USPS formatting rules:**
- Use all capital letters and no punctuation on envelopes (USPS OCR prefers this)
- Abbreviate street types: ST, AVE, BLVD, DR, STE
- Include the +4 ZIP code if you have it: `60601-3455`

## Complete Formal Letter Template

```
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[City, State ZIP]

[Full Date]

[Recipient's Name]
[Recipient's Title]
[Organization]
[Address]
[City, State ZIP]

Dear [Name]:

I am writing to [state your purpose clearly in one sentence].

[Provide supporting context, evidence, or details. Keep paragraphs short — 3–5 sentences each. Use bullet points for lists.]

I would appreciate [state the specific action you want]. Please contact me at [phone/email] if you have any questions or need additional information. I expect to hear from you by [date, if relevant].

Sincerely,


[Your Handwritten Signature]

[Your Typed Name]
[Your Title, if applicable]
```

---

## Quick Summary

- Sender's address (FROM) goes in the upper left; recipient's address (TO) goes below the date on the left
- Write out the full date (August 10, 2026, not 8/10/26) to avoid ambiguity
- Salutation: "Dear [Name]:" with a colon for formal letters; comma for personal letters
- Lead with your purpose in the first sentence — don't build up to the point
- Standard closing: "Sincerely," followed by four blank lines for your handwritten signature, then your typed name
- On envelopes: return address upper left, recipient address centered; all-caps and no punctuation is the USPS standard
