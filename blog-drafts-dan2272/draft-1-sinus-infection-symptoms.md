# Sinus Infection Symptoms: What They Feel Like, How Long They Last, and When to Act

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 21, 2026*

A sinus infection — technically called sinusitis — happens when the tissue lining your sinus cavities becomes inflamed and swollen, blocking the drainage channels and trapping mucus. The symptoms are distinct from a common cold, though the two often get confused. Knowing the difference tells you whether to rest and wait, or call your doctor.

## The 7 Classic Sinus Infection Symptoms

### 1. Facial Pressure and Pain

The hallmark symptom. You'll feel a dull, heavy pressure behind your cheeks, forehead, or around your eyes — the locations of the four main sinus cavities. The pressure worsens when you bend forward, lean over, or lie down. Unlike a tension headache, sinus pressure localizes to specific parts of your face rather than spreading across your whole head.

The most commonly affected areas:
- **Behind the cheekbones** (maxillary sinuses) — the most common site
- **Forehead, above the eyebrows** (frontal sinuses)
- **Between or behind the eyes** (ethmoid sinuses)
- **Deep in the skull, behind the nose** (sphenoid sinuses — rare, but causes a deep ache)

### 2. Thick, Discolored Nasal Discharge

Healthy mucus is clear and thin. With a sinus infection, discharge becomes thick and may turn yellow or green. The color comes from white blood cells fighting the infection — it doesn't necessarily mean bacterial vs. viral. You may feel the discharge dripping down the back of your throat (postnasal drip), which triggers coughing, especially at night.

### 3. Nasal Congestion

Your nasal passages feel blocked on one or both sides. You may not be able to breathe through your nose at all. This congestion also reduces your sense of smell (hyposmia) or eliminates it entirely (anosmia) — which in turn dulls your sense of taste.

### 4. Headache

Sinus headaches are specifically felt across the forehead or behind the eyes. They tend to be worse in the morning (when mucus has pooled overnight) and improve as the day goes on and drainage clears somewhat. The pain intensifies if you strain — from coughing, bending over, or bearing down.

### 5. Reduced or Lost Sense of Smell

With the nasal passages blocked, airborne scent molecules can't reach the olfactory nerve. This is temporary and usually resolves as congestion clears. If smell doesn't return after the infection clears, see a doctor — persistent anosmia after sinusitis is worth investigating.

### 6. Toothache or Jaw Pain

This surprises people. The maxillary sinuses sit directly above the upper back teeth. When they swell, the pressure can feel exactly like a toothache — particularly in the upper molars. If you have unexplained tooth pain without a dental cause, a sinus infection is the first thing to rule out.

### 7. Fatigue

Your immune system is working hard, and inflamed tissue throughout your head and face affects sleep quality. Most people with a sinus infection feel notably tired and run down even if their other symptoms seem mild.

## Sinus Infection vs. Common Cold: Key Differences

| Symptom | Sinus Infection | Common Cold |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Duration | 10+ days with no improvement | 7–10 days, improving after day 3 |
| Nasal discharge | Thick, yellow/green | Starts clear, may thicken briefly |
| Facial pressure | Prominent, localized | Mild or absent |
| Fever | Sometimes low-grade | Often present early |
| Sore throat | From postnasal drip | Often present early |
| Smell loss | Common | Occasional |

The biggest practical clue: **a cold improves after 5–7 days; sinus infection symptoms persist or worsen**. If your symptoms haven't begun improving by day 10, that points more to sinusitis.

## Acute vs. Chronic Sinusitis

**Acute sinusitis** lasts less than 4 weeks and usually follows a cold or upper respiratory infection. Most acute cases are viral and resolve on their own.

**Subacute sinusitis** lasts 4–12 weeks.

**Chronic sinusitis** lasts more than 12 weeks, often with lower-intensity symptoms that simply don't clear. Chronic cases are more likely to involve structural issues (deviated septum, nasal polyps) or allergies rather than infection.

**Recurrent sinusitis** means four or more acute episodes per year. If this describes you, an ENT evaluation is warranted to look for underlying causes.

## Viral vs. Bacterial Sinusitis

About **90–98% of acute sinus infections are viral** — meaning antibiotics won't help. The body clears viral sinusitis on its own within 10–14 days.

Signs that suggest bacterial sinusitis (and may warrant antibiotics):
- Symptoms that **worsen after initially improving** (double worsening pattern)
- Severe symptoms: high fever (above 102°F / 39°C), severe facial pain, swelling around the eyes
- Symptoms that persist unchanged beyond 10 days without any improvement
- Symptoms that are clearly getting worse, not just not improving

Even with bacterial sinusitis, many cases resolve without antibiotics. The American Academy of Otolaryngology guidelines suggest waiting 7–10 days before prescribing antibiotics unless symptoms are severe.

## When to See a Doctor Urgently

Most sinus infections are uncomfortable but not dangerous. Go to urgent care or an emergency room promptly if you have:

- **High fever** (above 103°F / 39.4°C)
- **Severe headache** not relieved by pain medication
- **Stiff neck** (may indicate meningitis)
- **Visual changes or pain around the eyes**
- **Swelling around the eyes or forehead** (may indicate the infection has spread to the orbit)
- **Confusion or altered mental state**

These can indicate rare but serious complications — orbital cellulitis, meningitis, or brain abscess — where the infection has spread beyond the sinuses.

## Treating Sinus Infection Symptoms at Home

For viral sinusitis, treatment focuses on symptom relief while your immune system does the work:

**Nasal saline rinse (most effective):** A neti pot or saline spray flushes mucus and debris from the nasal cavity. Studies consistently show this reduces symptoms and shortens duration. Use sterile or distilled water — tap water has caused rare but serious infections when used in nasal rinsing.

**Steam inhalation:** Lean over a bowl of hot water with a towel over your head for 10 minutes, 2–3 times per day. Adding eucalyptus oil is optional. This temporarily relieves congestion by loosening thick mucus.

**Stay hydrated:** Drinking 8–10 glasses of water per day keeps mucus thin and flowing, which aids drainage.

**Elevate your head during sleep:** Sleeping with your head raised 30–45 degrees (an extra pillow or wedge pillow) prevents mucus from pooling and improves nighttime drainage.

**Over-the-counter options:**

| Product | What it does | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Nasal saline spray | Moisturizes and loosens mucus | Safe for all ages, use freely |
| Decongestant (pseudoephedrine) | Reduces swelling in nasal passages | Avoid if you have high blood pressure; behind-the-counter in the US |
| Antihistamine (if allergy component) | Reduces allergic inflammation | May thicken mucus in some people |
| Ibuprofen or acetaminophen | Relieves pain and pressure | Take with food; follow dosing guidelines |
| Nasal corticosteroid spray (fluticasone) | Reduces inflammation | Works best with consistent daily use |

## How Long Does a Sinus Infection Last?

Most viral sinus infections resolve in **10–14 days**. Bacterial sinusitis with antibiotics typically improves within **7–10 days** of starting treatment.

If symptoms extend beyond 4 weeks, you may be dealing with subacute or chronic sinusitis, which requires a different treatment approach — often including prescription nasal steroids, longer antibiotic courses, or evaluation for structural issues.

## Quick Reference: Sinus Infection vs. Allergies

| Feature | Sinus Infection | Allergies |
|---------|----------------|-----------|
| Discharge color | Yellow/green | Clear |
| Fever | Sometimes | Never |
| Itchy eyes | Rare | Common |
| Seasonal pattern | No | Often yes |
| Duration | Days to weeks | Ongoing while exposed |

## Summary

A sinus infection is characterized by facial pressure, thick yellow or green nasal discharge, congestion, and fatigue. The key diagnostic clue is duration — a cold improves in a week; sinus infection symptoms persist or worsen beyond 10 days. Most cases are viral and don't require antibiotics. Nasal saline rinses, staying hydrated, and managing pain with ibuprofen or acetaminophen handle most cases effectively at home. See a doctor if symptoms persist past 10 days with no improvement, if symptoms severely worsen, or if you develop fever above 103°F, eye swelling, or a stiff neck.
