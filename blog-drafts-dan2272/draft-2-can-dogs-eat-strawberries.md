# Can Dogs Eat Strawberries? What's Safe, What's Not, and How Much Is Too Much

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 22, 2026*

Yes — dogs can eat strawberries, and most dogs enjoy them. Strawberries are non-toxic to dogs, low in calories, and contain fiber, vitamins, and antioxidants. That said, there are a few things to know about serving size, preparation, and which dogs should avoid them.

## Are Strawberries Safe for Dogs?

Strawberries are on the ASPCA's list of non-toxic foods for dogs. They contain no compounds that are harmful to dogs in normal amounts. Unlike grapes and raisins (which are seriously toxic to dogs) or xylitol-sweetened products (potentially fatal), plain fresh strawberries pose no toxicity risk.

**What strawberries contain:**
- Vitamin C — supports immune function
- Fiber — aids digestion
- Folate
- Potassium
- Manganese
- Omega-3 fatty acids (small amounts)
- Antioxidants including anthocyanins and ellagic acid

They're also about 91% water and roughly 33 calories per 100g — meaning even a generous serving adds minimal calories to your dog's diet.

## How Much Can a Dog Eat?

Treats — including healthy ones like fruit — should make up no more than 10% of a dog's daily calorie intake. The rest should come from a nutritionally balanced dog food.

| Dog Size | Weight | Max Strawberries Per Day |
|----------|--------|------------------------|
| Small | Under 20 lbs | 1–2 small strawberries |
| Medium | 20–50 lbs | 3–4 strawberries |
| Large | 50–100 lbs | 5–6 strawberries |
| Giant | 100+ lbs | Up to 8 strawberries |

Start with one or two and see how your dog responds, especially if they've never had strawberries before. Introduce any new food gradually to avoid digestive upset.

## How to Prepare Strawberries for Dogs

**Do this:**
- Wash thoroughly to remove pesticide residue
- Remove the green stem and leaves (they're non-toxic but unnecessary)
- Cut or slice strawberries — particularly for small dogs where a whole berry is a choking hazard
- For very small dogs or puppies, mash or puree strawberries before feeding

**Don't do this:**
- Feed strawberry jam, syrup, or preserves — these contain added sugar, and many contain xylitol, which is highly toxic to dogs
- Give chocolate-covered strawberries — chocolate is toxic to dogs
- Feed canned strawberries packed in syrup
- Leave stems on — not harmful, but they can cause mild irritation

The core rule: **plain, fresh or frozen strawberries only**. Anything processed or sweetened is off the table.

## Signs Your Dog Ate Too Many Strawberries

Strawberries contain natural sugar (fructose) and fiber. In excess, either can cause:
- Loose stools or diarrhea
- Upset stomach or vomiting
- Excess gas

These symptoms are temporary. If your dog ate a large quantity and shows severe or prolonged symptoms, contact your veterinarian.

## Which Dogs Should Avoid Strawberries?

**Diabetic dogs:** Strawberries are relatively low in sugar compared to many fruits, but they still contain sugar. If your dog has diabetes, check with your vet before adding strawberries as a regular treat.

**Dogs with kidney disease:** High potassium can be a concern for dogs with certain kidney conditions. Ask your vet.

**Dogs on calorie-restricted diets:** Even at 33 calories per 100g, if your dog is on a strict weight-management diet, your vet may prefer you stick to lower-calorie treats.

**Dogs with known fruit allergies or sensitivities:** Rare, but possible. If your dog has a history of food allergies, introduce strawberries in a very small amount and monitor for itching, hives, or GI symptoms.

## Can Dogs Eat Frozen Strawberries?

Yes. Frozen strawberries are a particularly popular treat in summer — many dogs enjoy the cold texture. Thaw slightly for small dogs or puppies to reduce choking risk. The same preparation rules apply: plain frozen strawberries, no added sugar or syrup.

## Can Dogs Eat Strawberry Leaves and Stems?

The leaves and stem are not toxic, but they can cause mild gastrointestinal irritation or discomfort in some dogs. There's no nutritional reason to include them. Removing the stem takes two seconds — just do it.

## Are Wild Strawberries Safe?

Wild strawberries (Fragaria vesca) — the small, intensely flavored varieties found in fields and gardens — are also non-toxic to dogs. The same serving size guidance applies. The greater concern with any foraged food is pesticide residue or contamination, so wild-harvested anything should be washed thoroughly.

## Strawberries vs. Other Dog-Safe Fruits

| Fruit | Safe for Dogs? | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-------|
| Strawberries | Yes | Low-calorie, good antioxidants |
| Blueberries | Yes | High antioxidants, excellent dog snack |
| Bananas | Yes | Higher sugar, limit portions |
| Watermelon | Yes (seedless) | Very hydrating; remove seeds and rind |
| Apples | Yes (no seeds/core) | Apple seeds contain trace cyanide compounds |
| Grapes | **NO** | Highly toxic — causes kidney failure |
| Cherries | **NO** | Pits, stems, and leaves contain cyanide |
| Avocado | **NO** | Persin is toxic to dogs |

Strawberries sit comfortably in the "safe and beneficial" category alongside blueberries — arguably better choices than bananas because of lower sugar content.

## The Bottom Line

Dogs can eat strawberries safely. Fresh or frozen, properly washed and sliced, they make a healthy low-calorie treat rich in vitamin C, fiber, and antioxidants. Keep serving sizes appropriate to your dog's weight (1–6 strawberries per day depending on size), never feed processed strawberry products with added sugar, and avoid if your dog has diabetes or kidney disease without first checking with your vet. For most dogs, a few strawberries a day is a treat they'll enjoy and a useful addition to their diet.
