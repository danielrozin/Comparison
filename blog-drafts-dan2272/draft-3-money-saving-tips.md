# 30 Money Saving Tips That Actually Work (Ranked by Impact)

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 23, 2026*

Most money-saving advice focuses on the wrong things. Skipping coffee saves $5 a day; renegotiating your rent or car insurance saves $100–$400 a month. The effort is similar. The payoff isn't.

These 30 tips are ranked roughly by impact — how much money you can realistically save per year. Start at the top. Work your way down.

---

## High-Impact Savings (Save $1,000–$5,000+ Per Year)

### 1. Switch to a High-Yield Savings Account

Standard savings accounts at big banks pay 0.01%–0.06% APY. High-yield savings accounts (HYSAs) from online banks currently pay 4.5%–5.0% APY. On a $20,000 emergency fund, that's the difference between earning $2 per year and $900–$1,000 per year. Moving money you already have takes 10 minutes.

**Action:** Open an account at Ally, Marcus by Goldman Sachs, or SoFi. Transfer your emergency fund.

### 2. Audit Your Subscriptions

The average American has 4–6 active subscriptions but thinks they have 2–3. Streaming services, gym memberships that go unused, SaaS tools auto-renewing, magazine subscriptions, cloud storage tiers — these add up to $150–$400 per month for many households.

**Action:** Check your credit card and bank statements for the past 3 months. Cancel anything you didn't use. Consider sharing streaming accounts with family members (where TOS permits).

### 3. Refinance High-Interest Debt

If you're carrying credit card debt at 20–28% APR, paying it down is the single highest "return" investment you can make. A $5,000 balance at 24% APR costs $1,200 per year in interest alone. Transferring to a 0% APR balance transfer card (many offer 15–21 months interest-free) or a personal loan at 8–12% APR saves hundreds immediately.

**Action:** Check balance transfer card offers. Calculate whether a personal loan beats your current APR.

### 4. Negotiate Your Car Insurance Premium

Car insurance rates are highly variable between providers for the same driver and vehicle. The national average premium is $1,800–$2,200/year. Switching providers typically saves $300–$600/year for drivers who haven't shopped in 3+ years. Loyalty is not rewarded in car insurance — the "loyalty discount" is usually smaller than the discount a competitor gives new customers.

**Action:** Get quotes from at least 3 providers every 1–2 years. Use your current policy's declarations page for comparison.

### 5. Meal Plan and Reduce Food Waste

The average American household throws away $1,500–$1,800 of food per year. Meal planning — deciding what you'll eat for the week before grocery shopping — dramatically reduces this. Buy ingredients that span multiple meals, use proteins and vegetables before buying new ones, and keep a "use it up" rotation.

**Action:** Spend 15 minutes each weekend planning the week's meals before you shop.

### 6. Cut Your Grocery Bill with Store Brands

Store brands (private label) are typically 20–30% cheaper than national brands for equivalent products. Studies consistently show that in many categories — canned goods, pantry staples, cleaning products, over-the-counter medications — store brands match national brand quality. A household switching from all-name-brand to primarily store-brand saves $50–$150 per month.

**Action:** Try store brand versions of 5 products you buy regularly. You'll find most are indistinguishable.

## Medium-Impact Savings (Save $300–$1,000 Per Year)

### 7. Use a Rewards Credit Card (and Pay It Off Monthly)

A cash-back credit card that returns 2% on all purchases earns $400/year on $20,000 in annual spending — free money if you pay the balance in full each month. Travel cards earn even more for frequent travelers. The key: pay the full balance monthly. Carrying a balance at 20%+ APR erases all rewards and then some.

### 8. Set Up Automatic Savings Transfers

You don't save what's left over — you spend what's left over. Automating a transfer to savings on payday removes the decision point. Start with 10% of take-home pay. Even $100/month compounds to $6,200 in 5 years at 4.5% APY.

### 9. Lower Your Phone Bill

The major carriers (Verizon, AT&T, T-Mobile) charge $60–$90/month per line. MVNOs (mobile virtual network operators) that use the same towers — Mint Mobile, Visible, Consumer Cellular — charge $15–$35/month with comparable coverage. Switching a family of 2 from $160/month to $50/month saves $1,320/year.

### 10. Use GoodRx or Generic Prescriptions

Generic prescriptions cost 80–85% less than brand-name equivalents on average. GoodRx is a free app that shows the lowest price for any prescription at nearby pharmacies — it frequently beats insurance pricing. Always check before filling.

### 11. Refinance Your Student Loans (If Private)

Federal student loans should generally not be refinanced privately (you'd lose income-driven repayment protections). Private student loans, however, may be candidates for refinancing if your credit score has improved since you originally borrowed. Dropping from 9% to 6% APR on $30,000 saves roughly $900/year.

### 12. Negotiate Your Salary

Employees who don't negotiate their salary at the time of hire leave an average of $5,000–$10,000 per year on the table over their career. Asking is nearly always safe — 87% of employers say a salary ask does not negatively affect their perception of the candidate. Research market rates on LinkedIn Salary, Glassdoor, and Levels.fyi before any negotiation.

### 13. Buy a Smaller Car Than You Think You Need

Monthly car costs (payment + insurance + fuel + maintenance) often run $600–$900 for a new midsize SUV. A smaller, reliable used car can cut this to $300–$400/month — a $3,600–$6,000/year difference. Reliability data from Consumer Reports and J.D. Power helps identify used vehicles with low maintenance costs.

## Everyday Savings That Add Up (Save $100–$300 Per Year)

### 14. Batch Your Errands and Drive Less

Combining trips reduces both fuel costs and impulse purchases made when you're already out. Fuel costs are roughly $0.15–$0.20 per mile; combining 3 separate trips into 1 saves 20–40 miles per week, or $400–$700/year in fuel for average drivers.

### 15. Buy Clothes Off-Season

Retailers clear seasonal inventory with 50–70% discounts. Buy winter coats in February, summer clothes in August. The items are identical to what you'd buy in-season; the savings are real.

### 16. Use Cashback Portals Before Online Shopping

Rakuten, Capital One Shopping, and similar browser extensions apply automatically to online purchases at thousands of retailers, returning 1–15% cash back. Activating this takes one install. Annual savings for moderate online shoppers typically run $100–$400.

### 17. Call and Negotiate Your Bills

Internet, cable, and some insurance bills are negotiable. Call and ask for the retention department — the team whose job is to keep customers from canceling. Mention competitor offers. Success rates are high; typical savings are $10–$40/month per service.

### 18. Pack Lunch 3 Days Per Week

Restaurant and takeout lunch averages $12–$18. A packed lunch costs $3–$5. Packing 3 days per week saves $25–$45/week — $1,300–$2,340/year. This is one of the few "small daily habits" that genuinely compounds to significant annual savings.

### 19. Use Your Library

Libraries provide free access to books, audiobooks (Libby/OverDrive), movies, magazines, streaming services (Kanopy, Hoopla), and digital newspapers. If you're spending $50+/month on books, streaming, or subscriptions these services cover, a library card replaces most of it for free.

### 20. Buy Used for Depreciating Items

Electronics, furniture, tools, kitchen appliances, and sports equipment depreciate sharply. A $400 stand mixer bought used on Facebook Marketplace for $120 performs identically. Same for tools, baby gear, bikes, and anything mechanical that doesn't wear out with typical use.

## Habit-Level Changes (Prevent Future Spending)

### 21. Implement a 24-Hour Rule for Non-Essential Purchases

Before buying anything over $50 that wasn't on your list, wait 24 hours. This eliminates roughly 80% of impulse purchases — most of which you simply forget about. For purchases over $200, extend to 72 hours.

### 22. Unsubscribe from Retail Emails

Retailer emails are engineered to create urgency and remind you of products you don't need. Unsubscribing takes one click per email. People who don't receive promotional emails spend an average of 15–20% less at those retailers.

### 23. Meal Prep on Sundays

Prepping proteins, grains, and vegetables in advance eliminates the "I'm too tired to cook" problem that drives expensive takeout orders. Even cooking one protein and one grain in bulk Sunday saves $50–$100/week in food costs.

### 24. Track Your Net Worth Monthly

People who track their financial accounts monthly make better financial decisions than those who don't — not because tracking itself saves money, but because seeing the numbers motivates behavior change. Free tools: Mint (now discontinued, alternatives: Copilot, YNAB), or a simple spreadsheet.

### 25. Automate Your Retirement Contribution

Contribute at least enough to your 401(k) to capture your employer match — that match is a 50–100% instant return on your contribution. If your employer matches 3%, not contributing that 3% means leaving a 3% raise on the table every year.

## Longer-Term Moves (Save $5,000–$20,000+ Over Time)

### 26. Reconsider Whether You Need Two Cars

For households where car 2 is occasionally used and rarely essential, the math often surprises: $400–$600/month in loan payment + insurance + maintenance = $5,000–$7,200/year to own a second car. Car-sharing, rental cars, and rideshares may be cheaper if you're honest about how often you actually use it.

### 27. Buy vs. Rent Your Home — But Only When It Makes Sense

The 5% rule: if annual ownership costs (property tax, maintenance, opportunity cost on down payment) exceed 5% of the home's value, renting a comparable property is often financially better. In high-cost cities, renting and investing the difference frequently outperforms buying. The break-even point is typically 5–7 years.

### 28. Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First

Before investing in taxable brokerage accounts, max out accounts that shelter money from taxes: 401(k) ($23,500 contribution limit in 2026), IRA ($7,000), HSA ($4,300 individual). The tax savings on maxing an HSA alone can exceed $1,000–$1,500/year for someone in a 25% tax bracket.

### 29. DIY Simple Maintenance and Repairs

Basic home maintenance — caulking, replacing light fixtures, painting, patching drywall, unclogging drains, replacing appliance filters — is learnable from YouTube in 30 minutes and saves $75–$200 per service call. DIYing 6 repairs per year saves $450–$1,200.

### 30. Build a 3–6 Month Emergency Fund Before Investing

This sounds like it delays saving, but it prevents the financial disasters that destroy savings: using credit cards for emergencies, withdrawing from retirement accounts (penalties + taxes can cost 40–50% of the balance), or taking high-interest personal loans. An emergency fund held in a high-yield savings account earns 4–5% while providing the stability that makes all other financial goals achievable.

## Where to Start

If you do nothing else from this list, do these three:
1. **Move your emergency fund** to a high-yield savings account (30 minutes, saves $500–$1,000/year in lost interest)
2. **Audit and cancel unused subscriptions** (30 minutes, saves $100–$400/month)
3. **Automate a monthly savings transfer** (10 minutes, builds wealth without willpower)

The rest compounds from there.
