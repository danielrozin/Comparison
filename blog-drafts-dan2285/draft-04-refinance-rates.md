# Refinance Rates in 2026: What to Expect and When to Lock

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 25, 2026*

Mortgage refinance rates in mid-2026 are averaging between **6.4% and 7.1%** for 30-year fixed refinances, and **5.8% and 6.6%** for 15-year fixed refinances, depending on credit score, loan-to-value ratio, and the lender. These are well off the historic lows of 2020–2021 but have declined from the peaks of late 2023, when 30-year rates briefly touched 8%.

This guide explains how refinance rates are determined, when refinancing makes financial sense in 2026, and what specific steps produce the best rate.

---

Rates change daily and vary significantly by lender and borrower profile. Use these as benchmarks:

|-----------|--------------------------|

| 15-year fixed refinance | 5.8%–6.3% |

**Important:** The rates above are averages for well-qualified borrowers. Your actual rate depends on credit score, loan amount, equity position, debt-to-income ratio, and the lender you choose.

## How Refinance Rates Are Set

Mortgage refinance rates are primarily driven by:

**1. The 10-year Treasury yield**
Most 30-year fixed mortgage rates track closely with the 10-year Treasury. When the 10-year yield rises (often during inflation or strong economic data), mortgage rates follow. The spread between the 10-year Treasury and the 30-year mortgage rate typically runs 1.5–2.5 percentage points.

**2. Federal Reserve policy**
The Fed doesn't set mortgage rates directly — it controls the federal funds rate, which affects short-term borrowing costs. But Fed signals about future rate direction strongly influence bond markets, which in turn influence mortgage rates. In 2025–2026, the Fed began cutting rates from the 2023 highs, which has gradually reduced refinance rates from their 8% peak.

**3. Your personal financial profile**
- **Credit score:** The biggest personal factor. A 760+ score gets the best rates; below 620 may not qualify for conventional refinancing
- **Loan-to-value (LTV) ratio:** Lower LTV (more equity) = lower rate
- **Debt-to-income (DTI) ratio:** Lenders prefer DTI below 43%
- **Loan purpose:** Cash-out refinances typically cost 0.25–0.50% more than rate-and-term refinances

## When Does Refinancing Make Sense?

The old rule of thumb was "refinance if you can drop your rate by 1%." A more accurate framework:

**Calculate your break-even point:**
```
Break-even months = Closing costs ÷ Monthly payment reduction
```

Example:
- Current payment: $2,100/month at 7.5% on a $300,000 loan
- New payment: $1,980/month at 6.75%
- Monthly savings: $120
- Closing costs: $4,500
- Break-even: $4,500 ÷ $120 = **37.5 months** (about 3 years)

If you plan to stay in the home longer than the break-even point, refinancing likely makes financial sense.

**When refinancing clearly makes sense in 2026:**
- You bought or refinanced at 7.5%+ and can now get 6.5% or lower
- You have an adjustable-rate mortgage that's about to reset higher
- You want to shorten your loan term (e.g., switch from 30-year to 15-year) to pay off the house faster
- You need to remove a co-borrower (divorce situation) or add one
- You want to eliminate FHA mortgage insurance by switching to conventional (if you now have 20%+ equity)

**When refinancing may not make sense:**
- You're only lowering your rate by less than 0.5% and closing costs are significant
- You're planning to move within 2–3 years
- You'd be extending your loan term significantly (refinancing from 20 years remaining to a new 30-year increases total interest paid even at a lower rate)

## 30-Year vs. 15-Year Refinance: The Trade-Off

| | 30-Year Fixed | 15-Year Fixed |
|--|--------------|--------------|
| Rate (July 2026 avg) | 6.7% | 6.0% |
| Monthly payment (on $300K) | $1,945 | $2,532 |
| Total interest paid | $400,200 | $155,760 |
| Better for | Cash flow flexibility | Long-term savings |

The 15-year saves dramatically on total interest but requires a higher monthly payment. A common strategy: refinance to a 30-year at the lower rate but make additional principal payments when cash flow allows — giving you the flexibility of a 30-year with the payoff speed of a 15-year.

## Cash-Out Refinance: What to Know

A **cash-out refinance** replaces your existing mortgage with a larger loan and gives you the difference in cash. Common uses: home improvements, debt consolidation, or major expenses.

Cash-out refinances cost 0.25–0.75% more than rate-and-term refinances because lenders view them as higher risk. Most lenders allow a maximum 80% LTV after the cash-out — meaning you can only borrow up to 80% of your home's appraised value.

In 2026, with home equity at record highs for many homeowners, cash-out refinancing is popular despite the rate environment. But if your existing rate is below 6%, carefully compare the total cost of a cash-out refi versus a standalone home equity loan or HELOC, which wouldn't change your primary mortgage rate.

## How to Get the Best Refinance Rate

**1. Check your credit score first**
A 760+ score gets the best rates. If your score is between 700–759, one or two months of credit improvement (paying down credit card balances) could move you into a better bracket.

**2. Shop at least three lenders**
Rate variation between lenders can be 0.5%+ on the same borrower. Compare:
- Large national banks (Wells Fargo, Chase, Bank of America)
- Credit unions (often lowest rates for members)
- Mortgage-specific lenders (Better.com, loanDepot, Rocket Mortgage)
- Local community banks

**3. Get a Loan Estimate from each lender**
Lenders are required by law to provide a standardized Loan Estimate within three business days of receiving your application. Use these to compare both rate AND closing costs on an apples-to-apples basis.

**4. Consider paying points**
Mortgage points (each point = 1% of the loan amount) let you buy down the rate. Each point typically reduces the rate by about 0.25%. Whether this makes sense depends on your break-even calculation.

**5. Lock the rate at the right time**
Rate locks typically last 30–60 days. Lock when you find a rate you're comfortable with — rates can move 0.25–0.5% in a week during volatile periods. Most lenders offer a float-down option (pay a small fee and you get the lower rate if it drops during your lock period).

## The Refinancing Process: Timeline

| Step | Typical Timeframe |
|------|-------------------|
| Application → pre-approval | 1–3 days |
| Home appraisal | 1–2 weeks |
| Underwriting | 1–3 weeks |
| Closing | 1–3 days from clear to close |
| Total average | **30–45 days** |

Average refinance closing costs run $2,500–$5,000 on a $300,000 loan (about 0.8–1.7%). Some lenders offer "no-closing-cost" refinances, which roll the costs into the loan balance or offset them with a slightly higher rate.

## Quick Summary

- 30-year refinance rates average 6.5–6.9% in mid-2026; 15-year rates average 5.8–6.3%
- Refinancing typically makes sense if you can drop your rate by 0.75%+ and you'll stay in the home longer than the break-even period
- Shop at least three lenders — rate differences of 0.5% on a $300K loan equal $100/month in payment difference
- Cash-out refinances cost 0.25–0.75% more and reset your equity clock; compare them against HELOCs before proceeding
- Lock your rate when you find a comfortable number — don't try to time the market
