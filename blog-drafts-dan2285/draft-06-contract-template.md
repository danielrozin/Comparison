# Free Contract Templates: The 8 You Actually Need

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 27, 2026*

A good contract doesn't need to be long or full of legal jargon — it needs to clearly state what each party is agreeing to do, what happens if someone doesn't do it, and when the agreement ends. These eight contract templates cover the situations that freelancers, small business owners, and landlords encounter most often, with plain-language explanations of what each section does.

**Important:** Template contracts are starting points, not substitutes for legal advice on high-stakes agreements. For contracts involving significant sums, intellectual property, or employment relationships, have an attorney review the final document.

---

## 1. Freelance Service Agreement

**Use this when:** You're providing a service to a client — writing, design, photography, web development, consulting, or any project-based work.

**Key sections to include:**

**Scope of Work:** Be specific. "Design a website" is too vague. Write: "Design a 5-page responsive website including home, about, services, portfolio, and contact pages. Design includes desktop and mobile versions. Does not include copywriting or SEO optimization."

**Payment Terms:** Specify amount, due date, and payment method. Include a deposit requirement (25–50% upfront is standard for new clients), milestone payments for long projects, and a late payment clause.

**Revision Policy:** Define how many rounds of revisions are included (typically 2–3) and what additional revisions cost.

**Intellectual Property:** State when ownership of the deliverables transfers to the client (typically upon receipt of final payment).

**Termination:** What happens if either party wants to end the project early? Who keeps the deposit? What deliverables are owed?

**Sample payment clause:**
> Client agrees to pay a deposit of 50% ($[AMOUNT]) upon execution of this agreement. The remaining 50% is due upon delivery of the final deliverable. Payment not received within 14 days of invoice will incur a 1.5% monthly late fee.

## 2. Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

**Use this when:** You're sharing confidential information — business plans, product ideas, client lists, financial data — with an employee, contractor, or potential business partner.

**Key sections:**

**Definition of Confidential Information:** Be broad but precise. Specify categories of information covered (trade secrets, financial data, client information) and exclude information that was already public before disclosure.

**Obligations:** What the receiving party must do (keep it confidential, use it only for the specified purpose) and must not do (disclose to third parties, use for competing purposes).

**Duration:** Most NDAs run 2–5 years for general business information; indefinitely for trade secrets. A perpetual NDA is hard to enforce but signals the seriousness of the obligation.

**Exceptions:** Information that the receiving party already knew, information they receive independently from a third party without confidentiality obligations, and information they're legally compelled to disclose (court order).

**One-way vs. mutual:** A one-way NDA protects the disclosing party only. A mutual NDA protects both parties — use mutual when both parties are sharing confidential information (common in partnerships or M&A negotiations).

## 3. Independent Contractor Agreement

**Use this when:** You're hiring someone as a contractor (not an employee) for a specific project or ongoing services.

**Critical distinction:** The IRS and Department of Labor scrutinize contractor vs. employee classification closely. Misclassifying employees as contractors can result in significant penalties, back taxes, and benefits liability.

**Key sections:**

**Contractor Status:** Explicitly state that the person is an independent contractor, not an employee, and that they're responsible for their own taxes, insurance, and equipment.

**Scope of Work and Deliverables:** Same specificity as the freelance service agreement above.

**Payment:** Hourly rate or project fee, invoicing schedule, and payment terms.

**Intellectual Property:** Specify who owns work product created during the engagement. Add a work-for-hire clause if you want the client to own all deliverables outright.

**No Exclusivity:** If the contractor can work for other clients simultaneously, state this explicitly to support the independent contractor classification.

## 4. Residential Lease Agreement

**Use this when:** You're renting a property to a tenant.

**State-specific warning:** Residential landlord-tenant law varies significantly by state. Some states require specific disclosures (lead paint, asbestos, mold history), specific notice periods for entry, and specific rules about security deposits. Use your state's template or have a local attorney review your lease.

**Key sections:**

**Parties and Property:** Full legal names of all tenants and the landlord; complete address of the rental unit.

**Term:** Start date, end date (or month-to-month designation), and what happens if the tenant stays after the lease expires.

**Rent:** Amount, due date (typically the 1st), grace period (if any), and late fees (typically 5% of monthly rent after a 3–5 day grace period, capped by state law).

**Security Deposit:** Amount (often 1–2 months' rent), conditions for return, and timeline for return after move-out (state law dictates the maximum).

**Occupancy:** Who is authorized to live in the unit. Any guests must leave after a specified period.

**Pet Policy:** Whether pets are allowed, species/breed/size restrictions, and any pet deposit or additional monthly rent.

**Maintenance:** Who is responsible for what — typically landlords handle major systems (HVAC, plumbing, electrical); tenants handle minor maintenance and report issues promptly.

## 5. Employment Contract

**Use this when:** You're hiring a full-time or part-time employee and want to define the terms of employment beyond the standard offer letter.

**Note:** Most US employees are "at-will," meaning either party can end employment at any time for any legal reason. An employment contract can modify this — clearly specify if you're creating an at-will or fixed-term arrangement.

**Key sections:**

**Position and Duties:** Job title, responsibilities, and reporting structure.

**Compensation:** Salary (or hourly rate), pay frequency, and bonus structure (if any). Specify that compensation may change per company policy.

**Benefits:** Health insurance, retirement plan, PTO policy, and other benefits — or reference the employee handbook.

**Intellectual Property:** Employees typically assign ownership of work created within the scope of employment to the employer. Specify exceptions (e.g., personal projects unrelated to company business).

**Non-Compete / Non-Solicitation:** Many states restrict or invalidate non-compete clauses. Non-solicitation clauses (prohibiting hiring of your employees or poaching clients) are more commonly enforceable.

## 6. Business Partnership Agreement

**Use this when:** You're starting a business with one or more partners.

This is the contract that most business partnerships regret not having when things go wrong. It should cover:

**Ownership split:** Percentage of the business each partner owns.

**Capital contributions:** What each partner is putting in (cash, assets, labor) to start and what happens if more capital is needed later.

**Decision-making:** What decisions require unanimous agreement vs. majority vote? Who can commit the business to contracts? Spending limits?

**Salaries and draws:** Are partners drawing salaries, profit distributions, or both? When?

**Dispute resolution:** How do you handle a deadlock? Is mediation required before litigation?

**Exit provisions:** What happens if a partner wants out? Can they sell to an outside party? Is there a right of first refusal for other partners?

**Dissolution:** Under what circumstances does the business dissolve, and how are assets distributed?

## 7. Simple Invoice Template

**Use this when:** You've completed work and need to bill a client.

An invoice is a contractual document. Include:
- Your business name, address, and contact information
- Client's name and billing address
- Invoice number (sequential)
- Invoice date and payment due date
- Itemized list of services or products with description, quantity, rate, and subtotal
- Total amount due
- Payment instructions (bank transfer, check, PayPal, etc.)
- Late payment terms
- Your signature or business stamp

---

## 8. Letter of Agreement (Simplified Contract)

**Use this when:** You need something more formal than a handshake but less formal than a full contract — small project with an existing client, simple one-off arrangement, or any situation where a full contract is overkill.

A letter of agreement is a short-form contract (often 1–2 pages) structured as a letter:

> "This letter confirms our agreement for [service] to be performed by [your name] for [client name]..."

It includes the same essential elements: who's doing what, when, for how much, and what happens at completion. The tone is more conversational but it's legally binding if both parties sign it.

## Where to Find Free Contract Templates

- **LegalZoom:** Paid service but offers some free templates; good for state-specific customization
- **Rocket Lawyer:** First document free, subscription for ongoing access
- **Law.com and FindLaw:** Free basic templates in their resource libraries
- **SCORE (score.org):** Free small business templates backed by the SBA
- **Your state bar association:** Many state bars publish free contract templates for common business situations

## Quick Summary

- Service agreement, NDA, contractor agreement, lease, employment contract, partnership agreement, invoice, and letter of agreement cover 90%+ of small business contract needs
- Specificity is the most important contract element — vague contracts fail in disputes
- For high-stakes agreements (large dollar amounts, IP ownership, employment), pay for attorney review
- State law governs many contract types, especially leases, employment, and consumer transactions — verify state-specific requirements
