# 15 Tax Tips That Actually Save You Money

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 23, 2026*

Most people pay more tax than they legally need to. Not because tax fraud is common, but because the US tax code offers dozens of legitimate deductions and credits that most filers either don't know about or don't claim correctly. These 15 tips cover the highest-impact actions, from contribution deadlines to deduction timing strategies, that reduce your actual tax bill.

---

## 1. Max Out Your 401(k) Contribution

The 2026 401(k) contribution limit is **$23,500** for employees under 50, and **$31,000** for those 50 and older (the extra $7,500 is the "catch-up contribution").

Every dollar you contribute to a traditional 401(k) reduces your taxable income by the same amount. If you're in the 22% federal tax bracket, maxing out your 401(k) saves you $5,170 in federal taxes alone — before considering your state tax savings.

**Action:** Increase your contribution percentage now, even by 1%. Many employers allow changes quarterly or monthly.

---

## 2. Contribute to a Health Savings Account (HSA)

If you have a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), you're eligible for an HSA. The 2026 contribution limits are **$4,300 for individuals** and **$8,550 for families** (plus a $1,000 catch-up for those 55+).

HSA contributions are triple-tax advantaged:
- **Pre-tax contributions** reduce your taxable income
- **Tax-free growth** inside the account
- **Tax-free withdrawals** for qualified medical expenses

Unused HSA funds roll over every year — there's no "use it or lose it" rule. After age 65, you can withdraw HSA funds for any purpose (like a traditional IRA), with non-medical withdrawals taxed as ordinary income.

---

## 3. Claim the Child Tax Credit and Child Care Credit

**Child Tax Credit:** $2,000 per qualifying child under 17 in 2026, with up to $1,700 refundable. This is a credit (dollar-for-dollar reduction in tax owed), not a deduction.

**Child and Dependent Care Credit:** If you paid for childcare while you (and your spouse, if married) worked or looked for work, you can claim 20–35% of up to $3,000 for one child or $6,000 for two or more children in qualifying care expenses.

Both credits have income phase-outs. Review your eligibility even if you've been ineligible in prior years — income changes after major life events often restore eligibility.

---

## 4. Deduct Student Loan Interest

If you paid interest on qualified student loans, you can deduct up to **$2,500** in student loan interest even if you don't itemize. This is an above-the-line deduction that reduces your adjusted gross income (AGI).

Income phase-out for 2026: the deduction begins phasing out at $80,000 AGI for single filers and $165,000 for married filing jointly, eliminating at $95,000 and $195,000 respectively.

---

## 5. Use the Right Filing Status

Your filing status (single, married filing jointly, married filing separately, head of household, qualifying surviving spouse) determines your standard deduction, tax brackets, and eligibility for many credits.

**Head of Household** is frequently missed: if you're unmarried and paid more than half the cost of keeping up a home for a qualifying person (dependent child, parent), you qualify for the head of household status — which has a higher standard deduction ($22,500 in 2026) and more favorable tax brackets than single filers.

---

## 6. Itemize If It Beats the Standard Deduction

The 2026 standard deduction is **$15,000 for single filers** and **$30,000 for married filing jointly**. Itemizing makes sense only if your deductible expenses exceed these amounts.

Common itemized deductions:
- **State and local taxes (SALT):** Capped at $10,000 per return (the TCJA cap, still in effect)
- **Mortgage interest:** On loan balances up to $750,000 (loans originated after Dec 2017)
- **Charitable contributions:** Cash and non-cash donations to qualifying organizations
- **Medical expenses:** Expenses exceeding 7.5% of your AGI

If you're near the threshold, consider **bunching deductions** — making two years of charitable contributions in one tax year to push above the standard deduction.

---

## 7. Harvest Tax Losses in Your Investment Accounts

If you have investments in a taxable brokerage account that have declined in value, selling them at a loss creates a **capital loss** that offsets capital gains dollar-for-dollar. Losses beyond gains offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year; additional losses carry forward indefinitely.

This strategy, called **tax-loss harvesting**, is most valuable in December before year-end. Watch for the **wash sale rule**: you can't claim the loss if you buy substantially identical securities within 30 days before or after the sale.

---

## 8. Claim the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

The EITC is one of the largest tax credits available and is frequently unclaimed. For 2026:

| Filing Status | Income Limit (approx) | Max Credit |
|---------------|----------------------|------------|
| Single, no children | $18,600 | $632 |
| Single, 1 child | $49,400 | $4,328 |
| Single, 2 children | $55,900 | $7,152 |
| Single, 3+ children | $59,900 | $8,046 |
| Married, 3+ children | $66,500 | $8,046 |

You must have earned income (wages, self-employment) to qualify. The credit is refundable — meaning you receive it even if you owe no taxes.

---

## 9. Deduct Home Office Expenses (If Self-Employed)

If you're self-employed and use part of your home **regularly and exclusively** for business, you can deduct home office expenses.

**Simplified method:** $5 per square foot, up to 300 square feet ($1,500 max deduction).

**Regular method:** Deduct the actual percentage of home expenses (mortgage interest or rent, utilities, insurance, depreciation) that corresponds to the office space.

W-2 employees working remotely cannot claim the home office deduction on federal returns under current law.

---

## 10. Contribute to a 529 Plan for State Tax Benefits

529 plan contributions don't reduce your federal taxable income, but **34 states** (as of 2026) offer state income tax deductions or credits for contributions. If you live in one of these states and have children, funding a 529 creates an immediate state tax deduction while saving for education.

Some states allow deductions for contributions to any state's plan; others require you to use an in-state plan. Check your state's specific rules.

---

## 11. Keep Records of Charitable Donations

Cash donations under $250 require a bank record or written communication from the charity. For donations of $250 or more, you need written acknowledgment from the charity before you file.

Non-cash donations (clothing, furniture, vehicles) over $500 require Form 8283. Donations of $5,000+ for a single item typically require a qualified appraisal.

Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs) from IRAs for taxpayers 70½ and older (up to $105,000 in 2026) count as Required Minimum Distributions without being included in income — an excellent tax-efficient giving strategy.

---

## 12. Time Deductions and Income Strategically

If you expect your income to be lower next year (retirement, job change), consider deferring income into next year and accelerating deductions into this year. If you expect income to rise, do the opposite.

**Common timing strategies:**
- Delay invoicing in December if you're self-employed (income received in January is taxed next year)
- Make January's mortgage payment in December (prepay mortgage interest)
- Make year-end charitable contributions before December 31

---

## 13. Deduct Self-Employed Health Insurance Premiums

If you're self-employed and pay for your own health insurance (and aren't eligible for coverage through a spouse's employer plan), you can deduct 100% of premiums for yourself, your spouse, and dependents. This is an above-the-line deduction that reduces AGI without itemizing.

---

## 14. File Electronically and Set Up Direct Deposit

Electronically filed returns with direct deposit are processed faster — typically within 21 days for refunds versus 6–8 weeks for paper returns. E-filing also reduces errors: the IRS accepts more than 90% of e-filed returns immediately, while many paper returns require manual review.

Use IRS Free File if your adjusted gross income is $79,000 or below in 2026 — it's free federal tax software through the IRS website.

---

## 15. Don't Miss the Retirement Contribution Deadline

Traditional IRA and Roth IRA contributions for the prior tax year can be made up until **Tax Day (April 15)**. This means you have until April 15, 2027 to make a 2026 IRA contribution. The 2026 limit is **$7,000** ($8,000 if you're 50 or older).

If you contribute to a traditional IRA and your income qualifies, the contribution may be deductible. Roth IRA contributions are not deductible but grow tax-free — make the contribution now and let the April deadline work as a natural reminder.
