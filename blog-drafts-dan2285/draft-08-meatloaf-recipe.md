# Meatloaf Recipe: Juicy, Flavorful, and Better Than Mom's

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 29, 2026*

A meatloaf that's dry or bland usually fails for one of two reasons: the wrong fat ratio in the meat, or no panade (a mixture of bread and milk that keeps the interior moist). This recipe fixes both problems. The result is a sliceable, moist, full-flavored meatloaf with a caramelized glaze — the version that makes people ask for seconds.

---

## Ingredients (Serves 6–8)

**For the meatloaf:**
- 2 lbs 80/20 ground beef (the fat matters — see below)
- 1 cup breadcrumbs (plain or panko) or 2 slices of white bread, crusts removed
- ½ cup whole milk
- 2 large eggs, lightly beaten
- 1 medium yellow onion, finely diced (about 1 cup)
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 2 tablespoons Worcestershire sauce
- 2 tablespoons tomato paste
- 1½ teaspoons kosher salt
- 1 teaspoon black pepper
- 1 teaspoon dried thyme
- ½ teaspoon smoked paprika (optional, adds depth)
- 2 tablespoons vegetable oil (for sautéeing the onion)

**For the glaze:**
- ½ cup ketchup
- 2 tablespoons brown sugar
- 1 tablespoon apple cider vinegar
- 1 teaspoon Worcestershire sauce
- ½ teaspoon garlic powder

---

## Why This Recipe Works

**80/20 ground beef:** Higher fat content means more flavor and moisture. 90/10 or leaner ground beef produces a dry, crumbly meatloaf. The fat renders during cooking and bastes the interior continuously.

**The panade (bread + milk):** This is the key to moist meatloaf. Breadcrumbs soaked in milk absorb and retain moisture, preventing the proteins from contracting too tightly as they cook. Without a panade, you get a dense, dry loaf.

**Sautéeing the onion:** Raw onion takes longer to cook than meatloaf and leaves crunchy bits inside. Sautéeing beforehand — until soft and translucent — eliminates this problem and mellows the onion flavor.

**Tomato paste and Worcestershire:** Both add umami depth and savory complexity without making the loaf taste like tomato sauce. These are the "why does this taste so good" ingredients.

## Method

### Step 1: Prep the Panade and Aromatics

Combine breadcrumbs and milk in a large mixing bowl. Stir and let sit for 5 minutes until the breadcrumbs absorb the milk and become paste-like. If using bread slices, tear them into small pieces before combining with milk.

In a small skillet over medium heat, warm the vegetable oil. Add the diced onion and cook, stirring occasionally, for 8–10 minutes until soft and translucent. Add garlic and cook 1 more minute. Remove from heat and let cool for 5 minutes.

**Why this matters:** Hot onion and garlic added directly to the ground beef will begin cooking the meat mixture unevenly. Let them cool.

### Step 2: Combine the Meatloaf Mixture

Add to the bowl with the panade:
- Beaten eggs
- Sautéed onion and garlic
- Worcestershire sauce
- Tomato paste
- Salt, pepper, thyme, and paprika

Mix with a fork until combined. Add the ground beef and mix with your hands until just incorporated. **Do not overmix** — overworking develops the myosin proteins in the beef, resulting in a tough, dense loaf. Mix until the ingredients are evenly distributed and stop.

The mixture should feel soft and hold its shape when pressed. It will be looser than you might expect.

### Step 3: Shape the Loaf

Preheat your oven to **350°F**.

Transfer the mixture to a rimmed baking sheet lined with aluminum foil (do not use a loaf pan — baking on a flat surface allows the exterior to develop a crust on all sides; a loaf pan traps steam and produces a wet exterior).

Shape into a loaf approximately 9 inches long by 4 inches wide by 2 inches tall, with even sides and a slightly domed top.

### Step 4: Make the Glaze

In a small bowl, whisk together ketchup, brown sugar, apple cider vinegar, Worcestershire sauce, and garlic powder. Taste — it should be sweet, tangy, and slightly savory. Adjust brown sugar or vinegar as needed.

**Apply half the glaze** to the top and sides of the meatloaf before it goes in the oven.

### Step 5: Bake

Bake at 350°F for 45 minutes. Remove from oven and brush with the remaining glaze. Return to oven and bake for an additional 15–20 minutes until the internal temperature reads **160°F** on an instant-read thermometer.

Total baking time: approximately 60–65 minutes.

The glaze should be caramelized, deeply colored, and slightly sticky when done.

### Step 6: Rest Before Slicing

**Rest for 10 minutes** before slicing. This is not optional — cutting immediately causes the juices to run out and leaves you with a drier loaf. Tent loosely with foil while resting.

Slice with a sharp knife to get clean portions. Use a spatula to transfer slices to plates — the baked-in shape won't hold together perfectly until it cools slightly.

---

## Internal Temperature Guide

| Temperature | Result |
|-------------|--------|
| 155°F | Slightly pink center, maximum juiciness (carryover will reach 160°F) |
| **160°F** | Fully cooked, the USDA standard for ground beef |
| 165°F+ | Overcooked, beginning to dry out |

Pull the meatloaf at 155–158°F. It will rise to 160°F during the 10-minute rest (carryover cooking).

## Meatloaf Variations

### Classic Turkey Meatloaf
Replace ground beef with 93/7 ground turkey. Add ¼ cup chopped fresh parsley and increase Worcestershire to 3 tablespoons to compensate for less fat-based flavor. Turkey meatloaf is leaner but drier — the panade is even more important here.

### Italian Meatloaf
Add ½ cup freshly grated Parmesan to the mixture. Replace thyme with Italian seasoning (1 teaspoon). Substitute marinara sauce for the ketchup glaze. Finish with mozzarella on top in the last 10 minutes.

### Mini Meatloaves (Muffin Tin)
Divide the mixture into a 12-cup muffin tin for individual portions. Cook at 375°F for 18–22 minutes until internal temperature reaches 160°F. These also freeze well after baking.

## Loaf Pan vs. Baking Sheet: The Definitive Answer

**Baking sheet wins.** Here's why:

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Baking sheet | Crust on all sides; better browning; less steam | Spreads slightly more |
| Loaf pan | Holds shape perfectly | Steamy interior; soft/wet exterior |

The crust that forms on the sides and bottom of a baking sheet-baked meatloaf adds flavor and texture that you don't get from a loaf pan. The loaf holds its shape fine if the panade ratio is right.

## Make-Ahead and Storage

**Make ahead:** Mix the meatloaf (without shaping), cover, and refrigerate for up to 24 hours. Shape and bake directly from the refrigerator — add 10–15 minutes to the baking time.

**Refrigerator:** Cooked meatloaf keeps for 3–4 days in an airtight container. Slice and reheat individual portions in a skillet with a splash of beef broth (prevents drying) or microwave at 50% power.

**Freezer:** Wrap individual slices in plastic wrap and freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat as above.

## Quick Summary

- Use 80/20 ground beef — leaner beef produces a dry loaf
- Make a panade (breadcrumbs soaked in milk) — this is the moisture secret
- Sauté onion and garlic before adding to the mixture
- Bake on a baking sheet, not a loaf pan, for a crust on all sides
- Apply half the glaze before baking, half after 45 minutes
- Cook to 160°F internal temperature; rest 10 minutes before slicing
