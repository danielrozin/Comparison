# American Credit Acceptance: What Borrowers Need to Know

*By Daniel Rozin | A Versus B | July 22, 2026*

American Credit Acceptance (ACA) is a subprime auto finance company that specializes in indirect lending through car dealerships. If your credit score is below 620 — or if you have a recent bankruptcy, repossession, or limited credit history — you may have been offered financing through ACA at a dealership. This guide explains how ACA loans work, what interest rates to expect, how to contact them with payment issues, and what your options are if you fall behind.

---

## What Is American Credit Acceptance?

American Credit Acceptance LLC, headquartered in Spartanburg, South Carolina, is a privately held consumer finance company founded in 2007. ACA focuses exclusively on **subprime auto loans**, meaning they approve borrowers that traditional banks and credit unions typically decline.

ACA doesn't lend directly to consumers. Instead, they purchase loan contracts from dealerships after you complete the car purchase. This is called **indirect lending**: you negotiate the car deal at the dealership, the dealer submits your application to ACA (and potentially other lenders), and ACA decides whether to buy the contract.

Once ACA owns your loan, all your payments go to them — not the dealership.

---

## How ACA Loan Terms Work

Subprime auto lending comes with significantly higher costs than prime financing. Here's what to expect:

| Feature | Typical ACA Range |
|--------|-------------------|
| APR | 18%–29% |
| Loan term | 36–72 months |
| Down payment required | Often $500–$2,000 |
| Vehicle type | Used vehicles primarily (some new) |
| Minimum credit score | No published minimum; approves 500s and below |

**Interest rate reality check:** A $15,000 loan at 24% APR over 60 months results in a monthly payment of approximately $430 and total interest paid of $10,800. Compare this to the same loan at 6% APR (available to borrowers with good credit), which would cost $289/month with $2,340 total interest.

Subprime auto loans can be the only path to vehicle ownership for borrowers recovering from financial hardship — but the total cost is dramatically higher than prime financing.

## Payment Methods ACA Accepts

- **Online:** ACH bank transfer (free), debit card (may have a processing fee around $3–$5)
- **Phone:** Call their payment line — same fee structure as online
- **Western Union / MoneyGram:** Cash payments accepted (fees apply)
- **Mail:** Check or money order to their payment address (allow 5–7 business days for processing)

**Important:** If you're mailing a payment near the due date, mail it at least a week early or pay by phone/online instead. Late fees typically range from $15–$30 and late payments will be reported to credit bureaus after 30 days.

## What to Do If You Can't Make a Payment

ACA offers **payment deferrals** for borrowers facing temporary hardship. A deferral moves one or two payments to the end of your loan — you still owe them, but you get a grace period now. Interest continues to accrue during the deferment.

To request a deferral:
1. Call ACA customer service before your payment is due
2. Explain your hardship (job loss, medical emergency, etc.)
3. ACA reviews your account history — if you've been paying reliably, approval is more likely
4. Deferrals are typically limited to 1–2 per 12-month period

**If you're more than 30 days late**, ACA will begin collection activity and may report the delinquency to the major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). After 60–90 days of non-payment, they may initiate vehicle repossession.

---

## ACA and Vehicle Repossession

ACA, like most subprime auto lenders, moves quickly to repossession once a loan is significantly past due. In most states, there's no required notice period before repossession — a repo agent can take the vehicle once you're in default (typically 30–60 days past due, depending on your contract terms).

**After repossession:**
- ACA will notify you that the vehicle was repossessed and give you a redemption period
- To get the car back, you typically must pay all past-due payments plus repo fees
- If you don't redeem the vehicle, ACA sells it at auction
- If the auction sale doesn't cover your remaining loan balance plus fees, you owe the **deficiency balance**
- ACA may pursue the deficiency balance through collections or a lawsuit

## How ACA Reports to Credit Bureaus

ACA reports to all three major credit bureaus monthly. If you pay on time, your account shows as current and helps build your credit score over time — one of the few genuine benefits of a subprime auto loan. If you miss payments, those negatives stay on your report for seven years.

**Tip:** Set up autopay through ACA's portal if your income is consistent. On-time payments are the single most impactful factor in credit score recovery.

## Should You Accept an ACA Loan?

If ACA is the only lender that will approve you, the loan may be necessary to access transportation. But go in with clear eyes:

- Calculate the total interest you'll pay over the life of the loan
- Keep the loan term as short as you can afford (shorter term = less total interest)
- Make every payment on time — it rebuilds credit and avoids penalties
- Refinance within 12–24 months if your credit score improves (even dropping 3–4% APR saves thousands)

ACA's business model works because many borrowers are in urgent need of a vehicle and have no other options. If you can wait 3–6 months and build credit before buying, the savings on a lower-APR loan will be significant.

## Quick Summary

- ACA is a subprime indirect auto lender operating through dealerships nationwide
- APRs typically range from 18–29%; total cost can be double the vehicle's purchase price
- Call 1-800-288-2227 for payments or hardship assistance; request a deferral before missing a payment
- ACA reports to all three bureaus — on-time payments help rebuild credit
- Refinance when your credit score improves to reduce monthly cost
