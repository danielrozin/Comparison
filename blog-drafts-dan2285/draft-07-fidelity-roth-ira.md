## What Is a Roth IRA?

A **Roth Individual Retirement Account** is a tax-advantaged retirement savings account funded with after-tax dollars. The key benefit: your money grows **tax-free**, and qualified withdrawals in retirement are also **tax-free**.

Contrast this with a traditional IRA, where contributions may be tax-deductible now, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.

**When a Roth IRA wins:**
- You expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement than you are today
- You're young with a long time horizon (decades of tax-free compounding)
- You want the flexibility to withdraw contributions penalty-free before retirement

**When a traditional IRA wins:**
- You're in a high tax bracket now and expect to be in a lower one in retirement
- You need the current-year tax deduction

**Important:** The $7,000 limit is combined across all your IRAs (traditional + Roth). If you have both, your total contributions to both accounts can't exceed $7,000.

**Income limits for Roth IRA contributions (2026):**

| Filing Status | Phase-out Begins | No Contribution Above |
|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Single / HOH | $150,000 MAGI | $165,000 MAGI |
| Married filing jointly | $236,000 MAGI | $246,000 MAGI |

If your income is above the limits, you can use the **Backdoor Roth IRA** strategy: contribute to a non-deductible traditional IRA (no income limits) and then convert it to a Roth IRA. Fidelity supports this process. Beware the **pro-rata rule** if you have other traditional IRA balances.

## Key Roth IRA Rules You Must Know

### Contributions Are From Earned Income
You can only contribute to a Roth IRA if you have **earned income** (wages, salary, tips, self-employment income). Investment income (dividends, capital gains, rental income) doesn't qualify.

**Exception:** A spousal IRA allows a non-working spouse to contribute to a Roth IRA as long as the working spouse has enough earned income to cover both contributions.

### 5-Year Rule for Tax-Free Withdrawals
Qualified distributions from a Roth IRA are tax-free only if:
1. You're 59½ or older, AND
2. At least 5 years have passed since January 1 of the year you made your first Roth IRA contribution (to any Roth IRA)

The 5-year clock starts at the beginning of the tax year of your first contribution — not the date of contribution. So if you open a Roth IRA and make a contribution in December 2026, the 5-year clock starts January 1, 2026.

### Contributions Can Be Withdrawn Anytime, Tax and Penalty Free
The **basis** (your after-tax contributions, not the earnings) can be withdrawn at any time, at any age, without tax or penalty. Only earnings are subject to the 10% early withdrawal penalty and taxes if withdrawn before age 59½.

### No Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)
Unlike traditional IRAs and 401(k)s, Roth IRAs have **no required minimum distributions** during the owner's lifetime. Your money can stay invested indefinitely. This makes Roth IRAs powerful for estate planning — beneficiaries who inherit a Roth IRA must take distributions, but the inherited funds remain tax-free.

After opening the account:

## Roth IRA vs. Roth 401(k): Which Is Better?

If your employer offers a Roth 401(k), you may be wondering how it compares:

| | Roth IRA | Roth 401(k) |
|--|----------|------------|
| 2026 contribution limit | $7,000 | $23,500 |
| Income limits | Yes ($150–165K for singles) | No income limits |
| Investment options | Very broad (Fidelity offers thousands) | Limited to employer's plan options |
| Employer match | No | Yes (on pre-tax 401k contributions typically) |
| Required minimum distributions | No (owner's lifetime) | Required at 73 (per SECURE 2.0, Roth 401ks now also exempt after 2024) |
| Flexibility | Can withdraw contributions anytime | More restrictions |

**Recommendation:** If your employer matches 401(k) contributions, capture the full match first — it's an instant 50–100% return. After that, fill your Roth IRA to the $7,000 limit, then return to the 401(k) or Roth 401(k) if you have more to invest.
